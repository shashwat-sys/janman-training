Section 1: Defining 'State' and Void Laws (Art 12–13)

The foundation on which all Fundamental Rights rest

Art 12

Definition of 'State'

For Part III purposes, "State" includes:

  • The Government and Parliament of India
  • State governments and legislatures
  • All local or other authorities within India or under the control of the Government of India

The Supreme Court has expanded this to include statutory corporations, government companies, and even some private bodies exercising public functions.

Art 13

Laws Inconsistent with Fundamental Rights are Void

All pre-constitutional laws (existing before 26 Jan 1950) that are inconsistent with FR become void. Any law made after commencement that abridges FR is void to the extent of the inconsistency. "Law" includes ordinances, orders, bye-laws, rules, regulations, notifications, and custom/usage.

⚖️ Key Case: A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)

First major FR case — the SC held that each FR operates in its own separate field. This was later overruled in Maneka Gandhi (1978) which adopted an expansive, interlinked reading of FRs.

💡 Important: Doctrine of Eclipse

Pre-constitutional laws inconsistent with FR are not absolutely void — they are "eclipsed" (dormant). If the FR is later amended to remove the inconsistency, the law revives. This applies to pre-constitutional laws only; post-constitutional laws that violate FR are void ab initio.

Section 2: Right to Equality (Art 14–18)

The cornerstone of constitutional justice — equality before law and equal protection

Art 14

Equality Before Law & Equal Protection of Laws

Two concepts: "Equality before law" (British concept — no one above the law) and "Equal protection of laws" (American concept — equals must be treated equally). Art 14 prohibits class legislation but permits reasonable classification.

Test for reasonable classification: (1) The classification must be founded on intelligible differentia — a clear distinction; (2) The differentia must have a rational nexus with the object of the legislation.

⚖️ E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu (1974)

Introduced the concept of "new equality" — arbitrariness is antithetical to equality. Art 14 strikes at arbitrariness in State action and ensures fairness and equality of treatment. This is the principle used to challenge arbitrary government decisions.

Art 15

Prohibition of Discrimination on Certain Grounds

The State shall not discriminate against any citizen on grounds only of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. No citizen shall be subjected to any disability or restriction on these grounds regarding access to shops, public restaurants, hotels, places of public entertainment, or places maintained out of State funds or dedicated to the public.

Important exceptions (Art 15(3)(4)(5)(6)): The State may make special provisions for women and children, socially and educationally backward classes or SC/ST, advancement of EWS (added by 103rd Amendment 2019).

Art 16

Equality of Opportunity in Public Employment

Equality of opportunity in matters of public employment. No discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, or residence. Parliament may prescribe residence as a condition. The State may make reservations for backward classes, SC/ST (Art 16(4)), persons with benchmark disabilities (2016 amendment), and EWS (2019 amendment).

Art 17

Abolition of Untouchability

Untouchability is abolished and its practice in any form is forbidden. The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act 1989 (amended 2015) implements this provision with criminal sanctions. Untouchability offences are non-bailable, non-compoundable, and cognizable.

Art 18

Abolition of Titles

No title shall be conferred by the State except military or academic distinctions. No citizen of India shall accept any title from any foreign state. Bharat Ratna and similar civilian honours are not "titles" under this article (Balaji Raghavan v. Union of India, 1996).

Section 3: Right to Freedom (Art 19–22)

Six freedoms, their reasonable restrictions, and protection against arbitrary arrest

Art 19

Six Fundamental Freedoms

  • Art 19(1)(a) — Freedom of speech and expression
  • Art 19(1)(b) — Right to assemble peaceably without arms
  • Art 19(1)(c) — Right to form associations or unions
  • Art 19(1)(d) — Right to move freely throughout India
  • Art 19(1)(e) — Right to reside and settle in any part of India
  • Art 19(1)(g) — Right to practise any profession or carry on any occupation, trade or business

Restrictions: Each freedom can be restricted on grounds specified in Art 19(2)-(6) — sovereignty, security of State, friendly relations with foreign states, public order, decency or morality, contempt of court, defamation, incitement to an offence.

⚖️ Shreya Singhal v. Union of India (2015)

The SC struck down Section 66A of the IT Act as unconstitutional — it violated Art 19(1)(a) as the restrictions did not fall within the permissible grounds under Art 19(2). Landmark free speech ruling in the digital age.

Art 20

Protection in Respect of Conviction for Offences

  • No ex post facto law (no person convicted for an act not an offence at the time of commission)
  • No double jeopardy (no prosecution and punishment more than once for the same offence)
  • No self-incrimination (no person shall be compelled to be a witness against himself)
Art 21

Protection of Life and Personal Liberty

No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law. Post-Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978), this procedure must be fair, just, and reasonable — mere existence of a law is not enough.

The SC has expanded Art 21 to include: right to dignity, privacy, education, health, livelihood, shelter, speedy trial, legal aid, clean environment, and many more rights.

⚖️ K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)

Nine-judge bench unanimously held that the right to privacy is a fundamental right under Art 21 of the Constitution. This landmark judgment struck down the earlier rulings in M.P. Sharma and Kharak Singh that had denied privacy as a FR.

Art 21A

Right to Education (Added by 86th Amendment 2002)

The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years. Implemented through the Right of Children to Free and Compulsory Education Act 2009 (RTE Act).

Art 22

Protection Against Arrest and Detention

  • No person arrested shall be detained without being informed of the grounds of arrest
  • Right to consult and be defended by a lawyer of choice
  • Arrested person must be produced before nearest magistrate within 24 hours
  • No detention beyond 24 hours without magistrate's authority

Exceptions for Preventive Detention: Art 22(3)-(7) allows detention without bail or trial under special laws. Parliament and State legislatures can make preventive detention laws for security, public order, and essential services maintenance.

💡 Writ: Habeas Corpus — Your Most Important Tool

A writ of habeas corpus ("produce the body") can be filed in the SC (Art 32) or HC (Art 226) when a person is illegally detained. It is one of the most powerful tools to challenge unlawful imprisonment. Even under preventive detention, the grounds must be communicated and a detenu has the right to make a representation.

Section 4: Right Against Exploitation (Art 23–24)

Abolition of forced labour, human trafficking, and child labour

Art 23

Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labour

Traffic in human beings, begar (forced labour without payment), and other similar forms of forced labour are prohibited. Violation is a punishable offence. The State may impose compulsory service for public purposes — this is not forced labour.

Implementing laws: Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act 1956 (ITPA), Bonded Labour System (Abolition) Act 1976, Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act 2012 (POCSO).

⚖️ People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982)

Expanded Art 23 to cover payment of less than minimum wage — any labour paid below minimum wage is "forced labour" violating Art 23. This case also established PIL as a tool for enforcing FR of the poor.

Art 24

Prohibition of Employment of Children in Factories

No child below the age of 14 years shall be employed to work in any factory, mine, or any other hazardous employment. The Child and Adolescent Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act 1986 (amended 2016) bans employment of children under 14 in all occupations and adolescents (14-18) in hazardous occupations.

💡 Art 23 is Enforceable Against Private Persons Too

Unlike most Fundamental Rights which operate only against the State, Art 23 also applies to private individuals. A private person practising bonded labour or trafficking can be prosecuted for violating Art 23 directly.

Section 5: Religious, Cultural & Educational Rights (Art 25–30)

Freedom of religion, minority rights, and protection of linguistic communities

Art 25

Freedom of Conscience and Free Profession, Practice and Propagation of Religion

Subject to public order, morality, and health, all persons are equally entitled to freedom of conscience and the right to freely profess, practise, and propagate religion. The State may regulate the secular activities associated with religious practice, and provide for social welfare and reform.

Art 26

Freedom to Manage Religious Affairs

Every religious denomination has the right to: establish and maintain institutions for religious and charitable purposes; manage its own affairs in matters of religion; own and acquire moveable and immoveable property; administer such property in accordance with law.

Art 28

Freedom from Attending Religious Instruction

No religious instruction shall be provided in institutions wholly maintained out of State funds. Attendance at religious instruction in other institutions receiving State aid is voluntary.

Art 29

Protection of Interests of Minorities

Any section of citizens having a distinct language, script, or culture has the right to conserve the same. No citizen shall be denied admission into any educational institution maintained by the State or receiving State aid on grounds only of religion, race, caste, language, or any of them.

Art 30

Right of Minorities to Establish and Administer Educational Institutions

All minorities (religious or linguistic) have the right to establish and administer educational institutions of their choice. The State shall not, in granting aid, discriminate against minority institutions. This right cannot be abrogated even by a constitutional amendment (T.M.A. Pai Foundation v. State of Karnataka, 2002).

Section 6: Right to Constitutional Remedies (Art 32–35)

The "heart and soul" of the Constitution — Dr B.R. Ambedkar

Art 32

Remedies for Enforcement of Fundamental Rights

The right to move the Supreme Court by appropriate proceedings for enforcement of FRs is itself a Fundamental Right and cannot be suspended except during an Emergency (Art 359). The SC has the power to issue directions, orders, or writs including Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, and Certiorari.

💡 The Five Writs Under Art 32 & Art 226

  • ▸Habeas Corpus — "produce the body" — challenges illegal detention
  • ▸Mandamus — "we command" — directs public authority to perform duty
  • ▸Prohibition — prohibits lower court/tribunal from exceeding jurisdiction
  • ▸Certiorari — quashes orders of lower courts/tribunals acting without jurisdiction
  • ▸Quo Warranto — "by what authority" — challenges occupation of public office
Art 226

High Court Jurisdiction (For Reference)

High Courts under Art 226 have wider writ jurisdiction than the SC — they can issue writs not just for FR violations but for "any other purpose" too. Art 226 is not itself a Fundamental Right, but it is a constitutional right. It is often preferred over Art 32 due to territorial convenience.

⚖️ Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India (1984)

SC held that letters and telegrams can be treated as PIL petitions. The Court appointed commissioners to investigate bonded labour conditions and issued detailed directions for rehabilitation. Established the principle that the Court can act on information from any source to protect FR of the poor.

Art 33 & 34

Restrictions on FR of Armed Forces & Martial Law

Art 33: Parliament may restrict FR of armed forces, police, intelligence services to ensure proper discharge of duty. Art 34: Parliament may by law indemnify acts done in connection with maintenance or restoration of order in area where martial law is in force.
