Section 1: Introduction to Directive Principles (Art 36–51)

Non-justiciable but fundamental in governance — borrowed from the Irish Constitution

Art 36–37

Definition and Application

Art 36 defines "State" for Part IV (same as Art 12 for Part III). Art 37 provides that DPSP are not enforceable by any court, but they are fundamental in governance. The State shall apply these principles in making laws. DPSP are non-justiciable — you cannot file a writ petition to enforce them directly. However, they provide guidance for legislation and can be used to interpret ambiguous statutes.

💡 Three Categories of DPSP

Socialistic Principles (Art 38-43A): Equal pay for equal work, right to work, education, public assistance, maternity relief, living wage, worker participation in management.
Gandhian Principles (Art 40, 43, 47-48): Panchayati Raj, cottage industries, prohibition of intoxicants, cow protection, village governance.
Liberal-Intellectual Principles (Art 44-45, 48A-50): Uniform Civil Code, early childhood care, environment protection, separation of judiciary from executive.

⚖️ State of Madras v. Champakam Dorairajan (1951)

First major FR-DPSP conflict. The SC held that in case of conflict between FR (Part III) and DPSP (Part IV), FR prevail. This led to the First Constitutional Amendment which added Art 15(4) to permit reservations, and Art 31A to protect agrarian reform laws.

Section 2: Key Directive Principles in Practice

The DPSP provisions most relevant to rights-based litigation and governance

Art 38

State to Secure a Social Order for Welfare

The State shall strive to promote the welfare of the people by securing and protecting a social order in which justice — social, economic, and political — shall inform all institutions of national life. Art 38(2) requires minimising inequalities in income, status, facilities, and opportunities.

Art 39

Certain Principles of Policy (Most Litigated DPSP)

  • Art 39(a): Adequate means of livelihood for all citizens
  • Art 39(b): Ownership and control of material resources distributed to serve common good
  • Art 39(c): Prevent concentration of wealth to common detriment
  • Art 39(d): Equal pay for equal work for both men and women
  • Art 39(e): Protect health and strength of workers, children
  • Art 39(f): Children given opportunities for healthy development
Art 39A

Equal Justice and Free Legal Aid (Added by 42nd Amendment 1976)

The State shall secure that the operation of the legal system promotes justice on a basis of equal opportunity, and shall, in particular, provide free legal aid, by suitable legislation or schemes or in any other way, to ensure that opportunities for securing justice are not denied to any citizen by reason of economic or other disabilities. This is the constitutional basis for the Legal Services Authorities Act 1987 and NALSA.

Art 40

Organisation of Village Panchayats

The State shall take steps to organise village panchayats and endow them with such powers and authority as may be necessary to enable them to function as units of self-government. Implemented through the 73rd Constitutional Amendment 1992 (Panchayati Raj).

Art 41

Right to Work, Education and Public Assistance

The State shall, within the limits of its economic capacity and development, make effective provision for securing the right to work, to education, and to public assistance in cases of unemployment, old age, sickness, and disablement. Courts have read Art 41 with Art 21 to derive enforceable rights to livelihood and social security.

Art 43

Living Wage for Workers

The State shall endeavour to secure, by suitable legislation or economic organisation, to all workers a living wage, conditions of work ensuring a decent standard of life and full enjoyment of leisure and social and cultural opportunities. The minimum wage laws give effect to this.

Art 44

Uniform Civil Code

The State shall endeavour to secure for the citizens a Uniform Civil Code throughout the territory of India. This remains one of the most contested DPSP — the SC has repeatedly urged the State to implement UCC (Mohd. Ahmed Khan v. Shah Bano Begum, 1985; Sarla Mudgal v. UoI, 1995) but it has not been implemented yet. The Uttarakhand UCC law (2024) is India's first.

Art 45

Early Childhood Care and Education

The State shall endeavour to provide early childhood care and education for all children until they complete the age of six years. (Pre-86th Amendment, Art 45 mandated free and compulsory education up to age 14 — this was moved to FR under Art 21A after 2002.)

Art 47

Duty to Raise Nutrition, Standard of Living and Public Health

The State shall regard the raising of the level of nutrition and the standard of living of its people and the improvement of public health as among its primary duties. Art 47 also requires the State to endeavour to bring about prohibition of the consumption of intoxicating drinks and drugs that are injurious to health.

Art 48A

Protection and Improvement of Environment

Added by the 42nd Amendment 1976. The State shall endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wildlife. Read with Art 51A(g) (Fundamental Duty to protect environment), this forms the constitutional foundation for all environmental legislation and PIL on environmental protection.

⚖️ Olga Tellis v. Bombay Municipal Corporation (1985)

The SC read Art 39(a) (right to livelihood) with Art 21 to hold that the right to livelihood is part of Art 21. Therefore, the right to life includes the right to livelihood. Eviction of pavement dwellers without notice or alternative accommodation violates Art 21. This is the classic example of DPSP being used to expand FR through judicial interpretation.

Section 3: FR vs DPSP — Conflict, Harmony, and Key Amendments

The most examined constitutional law question — and how courts have resolved it

FeatureFundamental Rights (Part III)DPSP (Part IV)
JusticiabilityEnforceable in courtNot directly enforceable
NatureNegative rights (restraints on State)Positive obligations on State
SuspensionSome can be suspended (emergency)Cannot be suspended
PriorityPrevail over DPSP (generally)Cannot override FR
AmendmentCan be amended (subject to Basic Structure)Can be amended freely
Art 31C

Protection of Laws Giving Effect to DPSP

Added by 25th Amendment 1971: Laws giving effect to Art 39(b) and (c) cannot be challenged as violating Art 14, 19, or 31 (now omitted). The 42nd Amendment extended this to ALL DPSP, but in Minerva Mills (1980), the SC struck down this extension as destroying the FR-DPSP balance that is part of Basic Structure.

⚖️ Minerva Mills Ltd v. Union of India (1980)

SC held that: (1) Harmony between FR and DPSP is part of the Basic Structure. (2) Parliament cannot give absolute primacy to DPSP over FR. (3) The 42nd Amendment's extension of Art 31C to all DPSP was struck down. The balance between FR and DPSP is itself a fundamental feature of the Constitution that cannot be destroyed.

💡 The Harmony Doctrine

Post-Kesavananda Bharati and Minerva Mills, the accepted position is: FR and DPSP are complementary, not contradictory. They must be read harmoniously. Laws implementing DPSP can restrict FR if they pass the proportionality test. Courts try to reconcile both rather than declare absolute primacy of one over the other.

Section 4: Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

Added by 42nd Amendment 1976 (10 duties) + 86th Amendment 2002 (11th duty on education)

The 11 Fundamental Duties under Art 51A — citizens' obligations to the nation:

Art 51A(a)Abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals, institutions, national flag and national anthem
Art 51A(b)Cherish and follow noble ideals which inspired our national struggle for freedom
Art 51A(c)Uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity and integrity of India
Art 51A(d)Defend the country and render national service when called upon
Art 51A(e)Promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood among all people of India; renounce derogatory practices
Art 51A(f)Value and preserve the rich heritage of India's composite culture
Art 51A(g)Protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers and wildlife
Art 51A(h)Develop scientific temper, humanism and the spirit of inquiry and reform
Art 51A(i)Safeguard public property and abjure violence
Art 51A(j)Strive towards excellence in all spheres of individual and collective activity
Art 51A(k)Provide opportunities for education to child (parent/guardian's duty) — added by 86th Amendment 2002

💡 Are Fundamental Duties Enforceable?

FDs are not directly enforceable in courts (no writ can be filed for breach). However: (1) Parliament can legislate to enforce them — e.g., laws punishing disrespect to national anthem (Prevention of Insults to National Honour Act). (2) Courts use FDs to interpret laws — if a law promotes FDs, it has better chances of being upheld. (3) In Bijoe Emmanuel v. State of Kerala (1986), the SC balanced FD to respect the national anthem with the FR of Jehovah's Witnesses not to sing it (religion-based) — a nuanced balancing exercise.

⚖️ M.C. Mehta v. Union of India — Environment FDs in Action

The SC has repeatedly invoked Art 48A (DPSP on environment) and Art 51A(g) (FD to protect environment) to issue directions for pollution control, closing polluting industries, and protecting forests. FDs strengthen PIL claims in environmental matters.

Section 5: DPSP in Contemporary Litigation and Rights Advocacy

How legal practitioners use DPSP to expand rights and challenge State inaction

DPSP as Constitutional Basis for Social Legislation

Most welfare laws in India derive their constitutional validity from DPSP. Key examples:

  • MGNREGA 2005 — right to work (Art 39, 41)
  • National Food Security Act 2013 — nutrition and food security (Art 47)
  • Legal Services Authorities Act 1987 — free legal aid (Art 39A)
  • Right to Education Act 2009 — education (Art 21A + formerly Art 45)
  • Child Labour Act 1986 — child protection (Art 39(f), 24)
  • Environment Protection Act 1986 — environment (Art 48A)
  • Maternity Benefit Act — maternity relief (Art 42)

Using DPSP to Enforce Rights in PIL

When a FR cannot be directly invoked, lawyers use DPSP with Art 21 to argue:

  • Art 39A + Art 21: Right to free legal aid as part of right to life
  • Art 41 + Art 21: Right to social security as part of dignified life
  • Art 47 + Art 21: Right to healthcare as part of right to life (Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Sabha, 1996)
  • Art 48A + Art 21: Right to clean environment as part of right to life (Subhash Kumar v. State of Bihar, 1991)

⚖️ Paschim Banga Khet Mazdoor Sabha v. State of West Bengal (1996)

The SC held that Article 21 includes the right to emergency medical treatment in State hospitals. A farm worker seriously injured in an accident was refused treatment in multiple government hospitals due to lack of beds. The SC read Art 21 with Art 47 (DPSP on public health) to hold that denial of emergency medical care violates Art 21. State ordered to pay compensation and improve hospital facilities.

💡 Art 39A in Action: Legal Aid as a Fundamental Right

In Hussainara Khatoon v. Home Secretary, State of Bihar (1979), the SC held that the right to free legal aid for an accused who cannot afford a lawyer is an essential ingredient of 'reasonable, fair and just' procedure under Art 21. This reading of Art 39A through Art 21 makes free legal aid a justiciable Fundamental Right — not merely a directive principle.
