Section 1: The Basic Structure Doctrine

The most powerful constitutional protection — limits on Parliament's power to amend

1973
Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala
13-judge bench · 7:6 majority

Holding

Parliament has wide power to amend the Constitution under Art 368, but cannot alter its "basic structure" or "essential features." The basic structure includes: supremacy of the Constitution, republican and democratic form of government, secular character, separation of powers, federal character, rule of law, judicial review, free and fair elections, fundamental rights, parliamentary system, and unity and integrity of India.

Why It Matters

This case overruled Golak Nath (1967) and established that even constitutional amendments can be challenged if they violate the basic structure. It is the most significant judgment in India's constitutional history — the foundation of judicial review of constitutional amendments. Every constitutional PIL invoking the basic structure doctrine traces back to this case.

1975
Indira Nehru Gandhi v. Raj Narain
5-judge bench

Holding

First application of the basic structure doctrine. The 39th Amendment immunised the election of PM from judicial review — the SC struck it down as violating basic structure (rule of law, free and fair elections). Free and fair elections are an essential feature of the Constitution that cannot be removed by amendment.

Why It Matters

Established that the rule of law and free and fair elections are part of the basic structure. No constitutional amendment can insulate electoral corruption from judicial review. Led directly to the Declaration of Emergency in 1975 (the darkest chapter in Indian constitutional history).

1980
Minerva Mills Ltd v. Union of India
5-judge bench

Holding

The 42nd Amendment's attempt to (1) give Parliament unlimited amending power and (2) extend Art 31C protection to all DPSP was struck down as violating basic structure. Judicial review and the balance between FR and DPSP are both essential features of the Constitution. Parliament cannot make itself supreme over the Constitution.

Why It Matters

Affirmed that judicial review is part of basic structure and cannot be removed. Restored the FR-DPSP balance that the Emergency-era 42nd Amendment had tried to destroy. Remains the definitive statement on the limits of Parliament's amending power.

Section 2: The Expanding Horizons of Article 21

How the SC transformed "life and personal liberty" into a comprehensive rights umbrella

1978
Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India
7-judge bench

Holding

Overruled A.K. Gopalan (1950). Held that: (1) FRs are not isolated silos — they must be read together; (2) "Procedure established by law" under Art 21 must be fair, just, and reasonable — not mere law-on-paper; (3) The right to travel abroad is part of Art 21; (4) The golden triangle — Art 14, 19, and 21 — must be read together.

Why It Matters

The watershed judgment that opened up Art 21 to include dozens of rights beyond literal "life and liberty." Set the stage for subsequent recognition of rights to livelihood, health, privacy, dignity, education, clean environment, and more — all under Art 21.

2017
K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (Privacy)
9-judge bench · Unanimous

Holding

Privacy is a fundamental right under Art 21. It inheres in each individual as a natural right and encompasses the right to be let alone, informational self-determination, and decisional autonomy. Overruled M.P. Sharma (1954) and Kharak Singh (1962). Any infringement of privacy must meet the triple test: legality, legitimate aim, and proportionality.

Why It Matters

Foundation for all digital rights litigation — Aadhaar challenges, data protection, surveillance. Challenged the constitutionality of the Aadhaar Act (subsequently upheld with modifications in Puttaswamy II, 2018). The "proportionality" test from this judgment is now used to evaluate all privacy-affecting laws.

Other Key Art 21 Rights Recognised by the SC

  • Right to livelihood — Olga Tellis (1985)
  • Right to emergency medical care — Paschim Banga (1996)
  • Right to speedy trial — Hussainara Khatoon (1979)
  • Right to free legal aid — M.H. Hoskot (1978)
  • Right to shelter — Shantistar Builders (1990)
  • Right to clean environment — Subhash Kumar (1991)
  • Right to dignity — Francis Coralie Mullin (1981)
  • Right to education (before Art 21A) — Unni Krishnan (1993)
  • Right to reputation — MC Mehta v. Union of India (1987)

Section 3: Equality Jurisprudence and Reservations

The Art 14-15-16 trilogy — from Champakam to Janhit Abhiyan

1992
Indra Sawhney v. Union of India (Mandal)
9-judge bench

Holding

The Mandal Commission OBC reservation (27%) was upheld. Key principles: (1) Total reservations cannot exceed 50% (the "50% rule"); (2) Creamy layer must be excluded from OBC reservations; (3) Reservation in promotions is not permitted under Art 16(4) (subsequently qualified by 77th Amendment and M. Nagaraj); (4) Art 16(4) is not exhaustive of reservation power — Art 16(1) also permits reservation-like measures.

Why It Matters

The foundational reservation judgment. The 50% cap and creamy layer exclusion remain law. Any new reservation scheme must navigate these principles. The EWS reservation (103rd Amendment 2019) breaches the 50% cap for the first time — upheld in Janhit Abhiyan v. UoI (2022) as valid because it operates as a separate compartment.

2022
Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India (EWS)
5-judge bench · 3:2

Holding

The 103rd Constitutional Amendment providing 10% EWS reservation was upheld by a 3:2 majority. The exclusion of SC/ST/OBC from EWS reservation does not violate Art 14 or basic structure. EWS operates as a separate category beyond the 50% ceiling set in Indra Sawhney.

Why It Matters

First time the 50% ceiling has been breached for a government-created reservation. The minority opinion (Justices Bhat and Trivedi) argued that excluding SC/ST/OBC from EWS violates Art 14 and the basic structure equality provisions. A deeply contested judgment relevant for ongoing reservation litigation.

Section 4: Recent Supreme Court Judgments (2019–2024)

Developments in constitutional law that every practitioner must know

2023
Supriyo @ Supriya Chakraborty v. Union of India
5-judge bench

Holding

The SC refused to recognise a constitutional right to same-sex marriage. Marriage is not a FR under the Constitution. The right to unionise / form a legal relationship may exist under Art 21 but the Court left this for Parliament. Struck down the challenge to special marriage laws on this ground. Directed formation of a committee to consider rights of queer persons.

Why It Matters

While the Court did not recognise same-sex marriage, it reaffirmed the Navtej Singh Johar (2018) reading of Art 21 as covering sexuality and intimacy. The debate on queer rights continues in Parliament. Practitioners working on LGBTQ+ rights must understand both Johar and Supriyo.

2024
Association for Democratic Reforms v. Union of India (Electoral Bonds)
5-judge bench · Unanimous

Holding

The Electoral Bond Scheme was struck down unanimously as unconstitutional. Voters have a fundamental right to information (Art 19(1)(a)) about political funding. Anonymous electoral bonds violated this right. The SBI was directed to disclose all bond data to the Election Commission immediately.

Why It Matters

Landmark application of the right to information under Art 19(1)(a). Established that political funding is a matter of public concern and voters have a constitutional right to know. Shows how FR (Art 19) can be used to challenge government policy that undermines democratic transparency.

2024
Property Owners Association v. State of Maharashtra
9-judge bench

Holding

9-judge bench addressed the scope of Art 39(b) (material resources of the community). By a 7:2 majority, held that ALL privately owned material resources are NOT automatically "material resources of the community" under Art 39(b) — the State cannot acquire all private property in the name of redistribution without adequate compensation. Overruled minority view in Sanjeev Coke Manufacturing Co (1982).

Why It Matters

Significant limitation on Art 39(b)-based legislation. Governments cannot expropriate all private resources in the name of public welfare. Relevant for land acquisition, natural resource allocation, and property rights disputes.

Section 5: Public Interest Litigation (PIL) — Filing and Drafting

How to bring constitutional rights violations before the Supreme Court and High Courts

What is PIL and Where to File

Supreme Court (Art 32): For violations of Fundamental Rights — original jurisdiction, no territorial limit. File when: the issue affects the public at large, lower courts cannot provide adequate relief, or there is systemic state failure.
High Court (Art 226): For FR violations AND "any other purpose" — wider than Art 32. Preferred for state-specific issues, faster relief, territorial convenience. HC can issue all 5 writs plus any direction or order.

💡 Locus Standi in PIL — Who Can File?

Unlike ordinary litigation (only aggrieved party), PIL allows any public-spirited person to file on behalf of those who cannot access courts. The SC has even accepted: letters to judges, postcards, newspaper reports, and telegram as PIL petitions. Courts appoint commissioners and amicus curiae to assist. There is no court fee for PIL petitions under Art 32.

Steps to File a PIL in the Supreme Court:

1

Identify the FR Violation

Which Fundamental Right is being violated? By whom (State/private body exercising public function)? Is it systemic or individual? PIL is appropriate for widespread, systemic violations affecting the poor and marginalised.

2

Gather Evidence and Affidavits

Collect: affidavits from affected persons, RTI responses, government reports, news reports, photographs, videos, expert opinions. The SC often relies on socio-economic data — NFHS reports, Census data, NSSO surveys.

3

Draft the Writ Petition

Structure: (1) Party details; (2) Synopsis & list of dates; (3) Facts in chronological order; (4) Grounds — which FRs are violated and why; (5) Questions of law; (6) Prayers (what relief you seek — writ, direction, compensation). File in prescribed format with SC Registry.

4

Filing and Preliminary Hearing

File with SC Registry (New Delhi) or e-file online. Pay court fee (₹50 for WP, free for PIL filed as letter petition). The matter is listed before a vacation bench or a bench dealing with similar matters. Be prepared for preliminary objections on maintainability and locus.

5

Interim Relief and Final Directions

At the first hearing, argue for interim directions. SC can: issue interim orders, appoint commissioners, direct production of records, and order status reports. Most PIL relief comes through monitoring and compliance — the case continues until systemic change is demonstrated.

1984
Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India
PIL · Letters as petitions

Holding

Letters written to the SC about bonded labour in stone quarries were treated as PIL petitions. The Court appointed commissioners to investigate, ordered rehabilitation under the Bonded Labour (Abolition) Act 1976, and continued monitoring. Established that courts can act on any communication about FR violations of the poor.

Why It Matters for Practitioners

Any Janman team member can write a detailed letter to the Chief Justice of India about systematic violations of labour rights, bonded labour, untouchability, or child labour in Bihar. If facts are compelling, the SC may treat it as a PIL. Document everything — the first step is getting the Court's attention.

Section 6: Strategic Constitutional Litigation — A Practitioner's Toolkit

Advanced techniques for using constitutional law to achieve systemic change

Combining FRs for Maximum Effect

  • Art 14 + Art 21: Arbitrary denial of welfare scheme benefits without hearing — due process violation
  • Art 17 + Art 21: Untouchability-based exclusion from MGNREGA or PDS — dignity violation
  • Art 19(1)(a) + Art 21: Criminalisation of dissent or suppression of whistleblowers
  • Art 23 + Art 21: Bonded labour, trafficking, manual scavenging — enforce with Art 21 compensation
  • Art 32 + DPSP: PIL arguing State failure to implement a scheme (e.g., ICDS, Mid-Day Meal) violates Art 21

Compensation Under Art 32/226

The SC and HCs can award monetary compensation for constitutional torts — violations of FR by the State. Key cases:

  • Rudul Sah v. State of Bihar (1983) — first case of compensation for illegal detention under Art 32
  • Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa (1993) — compensation for custodial death; constitutional tort doctrine
  • DK Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997) — guidelines for arrest; compensation for violation
Bihar-Specific
Patna High Court under Art 226 — Your Primary Forum
Art 226 · Wider than Art 32

For Bihar-Based Rights Violations

The Patna High Court is the primary forum for constitutional rights enforcement in Bihar. Under Art 226, the Patna HC can issue writs for FR violations AND for "any other purpose" — broader than the SC's Art 32 jurisdiction. Key PIL areas before Patna HC: prison conditions, undertrial detention, bonded labour, manual scavenging, child rights, welfare scheme non-implementation.

Janman Litigation Strategy

File at Patna HC first — faster disposal for Bihar matters, territorial connection. Escalate to SC for matters of national significance or when HC fails to act. The Patna HC Legal Services Committee and Bihar SLSA provide free legal aid for PIL work. Janman legal team can assist with strategy and drafting.
