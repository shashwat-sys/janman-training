The SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act ‚Äî What Every PLV Must Know

Who is this module for? This module is designed for Paralegal Volunteers (PLVs) working in communities where Scheduled Caste and Scheduled Tribe persons face caste-based violence. You do not need a law degree ‚Äî you need to understand the key protections the law provides, what counts as an atrocity, and what your role is when an incident happens. This module builds the foundation. Levels 2 and 3 will teach you exactly what to DO.

Topic 1 ‚Äî Why This Law Exists ‡§µ‡§ø‡§∑‡§Ø 1 ‚Äî ‡§Ø‡§π ‡§ï‡§æ‡§®‡•Ç‡§® ‡§ï‡•ç‡§Ø‡•ã‡§Ç ‡§¨‡§®‡§æ

The Constitutional Promise: Article 17 of the Indian Constitution abolishes untouchability completely. It says the practice of untouchability is forbidden and that enforcing it in any form is an offence punishable by law. This was a landmark promise made when India became a Republic in 1950.

The Gap Between Promise and Reality: Despite Article 17, caste-based violence continued ‚Äî people were stripped and paraded, their homes burned, their land grabbed, their children denied access to schools, and their women subjected to sexual violence ‚Äî specifically because of their caste. Ordinary criminal law (IPC/BNS) was not enough because it did not recognise the caste motive as an aggravating factor.

The SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989: Parliament passed this Act to fill the gap. It creates special offences that apply specifically when a non-SC/ST person commits acts of humiliation, violence, or exploitation against an SC or ST person because of their caste or tribal identity. The Act was significantly strengthened by amendments in 2015 and 2016.
Key Fact The full name is "The Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989." It is commonly called the "POA Act" or "Atrocities Act." In Hindi it is known as ‡§Ö‡§®‡•Å‡§∏‡•Ç‡§ö‡§ø‡§§ ‡§ú‡§æ‡§§‡§ø ‡§è‡§µ‡§Ç ‡§Ö‡§®‡•Å‡§∏‡•Ç‡§ö‡§ø‡§§ ‡§ú‡§®‡§ú‡§æ‡§§‡§ø (‡§Ö‡§§‡•ç‡§Ø‡§æ‡§ö‡§æ‡§∞ ‡§®‡§ø‡§µ‡§æ‡§∞‡§£) ‡§Ö‡§ß‡§ø‡§®‡§ø‡§Ø‡§Æ, 1989.
Scenario ‚Äî Understanding the Purpose
Why ordinary law isn't enough
Ramkhelawan (from a Dalit community in Bihar) is beaten by a dominant-caste landlord after he demands the minimum wage he is legally owed. The landlord also says: "Tum log apni aukaat mein raho" (stay in your place, people like you).

Under ordinary law (IPC/BNS): This is assault. The police might not register an FIR, and if they do, it's treated as a simple fight. No special compensation. No mandatory investigation by a senior officer. No Special Court.

Under the POA Act: The beating, combined with the caste-based humiliation, is an atrocity offence. The FIR must be registered immediately without a preliminary inquiry. A Deputy SP or above must investigate. Ramkhelawan gets interim compensation within 3-7 days. The case goes to a Special Court. The accused cannot get anticipatory bail.

Section 3 of the POA Act lists the specific offences that count as "atrocities." The most important rule: the offender must be a non-SC/ST person, and the victim must be an SC or ST person. A crime committed between two SC persons does not attract the POA Act (though it is still punishable under ordinary criminal law).

‚ö† Critical Rule ‚Äî The Caste Motive Not every crime against an SC/ST person is an atrocity under the POA Act. The offence must be committed by a non-SC/ST person against an SC/ST person on account of their caste or tribal identity. The Supreme Court (Khuman Singh v. State of MP, 2019) clarified that the caste-based motive must be present. In practice, courts often infer motive from the circumstances ‚Äî a PLV should document all caste-related remarks or context at the time of incident.

Topic 3 ‚Äî Who Is Protected? ‡§µ‡§ø‡§∑‡§Ø 3 ‚Äî ‡§ï‡§æ‡§®‡•Ç‡§® ‡§ï‡§ø‡§∏‡§ï‡•Ä ‡§∞‡§ï‡•ç‡§∑‡§æ ‡§ï‡§∞‡§§‡§æ ‡§π‡•à?

Scheduled Castes (‡§Ö‡§®‡•Å‡§∏‡•Ç‡§ö‡§ø‡§§ ‡§ú‡§æ‡§§‡§ø): A person listed in the Presidential Order for Scheduled Castes applicable to their state. In Bihar, this includes communities like Chamar, Dusadh (Paswan), Musahar, Nat, Dhobi, and many others. The list varies by state.

Scheduled Tribes (‡§Ö‡§®‡•Å‡§∏‡•Ç‡§ö‡§ø‡§§ ‡§ú‡§®‡§ú‡§æ‡§§‡§ø): A person listed in the Presidential Order for Scheduled Tribes for their state. In Bihar and Jharkhand, this includes Santhal, Oraon, Munda, Ho, and others.

How to confirm SC/ST status: The victim should have a caste certificate issued by a competent authority (Tehsildar/SDO). However, the law does not require the certificate to be present at the time of filing the FIR ‚Äî the PLV can help gather it later as part of the investigation.
!
The Offender Must Be Non-SC/ST If the offender is also an SC or ST person, the POA Act does not apply. The case is registered under IPC/BNS instead. This is one of the most common reasons police use to avoid registering an FIR under the POA Act ‚Äî always be alert to this argument and check whether the accused really is SC/ST.
!
Public Servants Have Separate Liability Section 4 of the POA Act imposes a specific duty on public servants who wilfully neglect their duties under the Act (e.g., a police officer who refuses to register an FIR, or an SDO who doesn't disburse Rule 12 compensation). They can be prosecuted under Section 4.

Topic 4 ‚Äî The PLV's Legal Mandate ‡§µ‡§ø‡§∑‡§Ø 4 ‚Äî PLV ‡§ï‡•Ä ‡§ï‡§æ‡§®‡•Ç‡§®‡•Ä ‡§≠‡•Ç‡§Æ‡§ø‡§ï‡§æ

What the Law and Supreme Court Say About PLVs

  • NALSA v. Union of India (2013) ‚Äî The Supreme Court directed that PLVs trained in the POA Act must be deployed in every atrocity-prone district to assist SC/ST victims in FIR registration, documentation, and Rule 12 compensation claims.
  • PLVs empanelled with DLSA have official status ‚Äî they are legal representatives for the purposes of accessing victims, accompanying them to police stations, and advocating before authorities.
  • A PLV cannot appear in court (only lawyers can), but can help the victim understand court proceedings, communicate with the DLSA-empanelled lawyer, and follow up on case status.
  • A PLV's role begins at the moment an incident is reported ‚Äî documenting the incident, supporting the victim, and initiating contact with the DLSA are all part of the PLV's mandate.
  • PLVs are entitled to witness protection under Section 15A of the POA Act if they face threats from the accused or their associates because of their work.
Scenario ‚Äî PLV in Action
What a PLV should do when an atrocity is reported
Sunita Devi (SC community, Gaya district) comes to PLV Shanti with bruises and reports that the landlord's son beat her and said "Chamar ki aukaat" when she asked for her wages.

Shanti's immediate responsibilities:
1. Listen and believe ‚Äî do not interrogate Sunita about whether she "provoked" the incident.
2. Document the incident in a written diary: date, time, place, what was said, names of witnesses.
3. Take or help take photographs of injuries (with Sunita's consent).
4. Accompany Sunita to the police station to help register the FIR ‚Äî remind the SHO that a preliminary inquiry is not permitted and the FIR must be registered under the POA Act immediately.
5. Call the DLSA to inform them of the incident and request immediate legal aid and interim relief under Rule 12.
This is exactly what Levels 2 and 3 will train you to do in detail.

Knowledge Check ‚Äî Level 1

10 questions ¬∑ Pass at 70% ¬∑ Explanations shown after submission
Q1Which Article of the Indian Constitution abolishes untouchability?
Article 17 specifically abolishes untouchability and makes enforcing it in any form a punishable offence. It is the constitutional foundation on which the SC/ST (POA) Act is built.
Q2Under the POA Act, who MUST the offender be for an act to count as an atrocity?
The POA Act explicitly requires that the offender be a non-SC/ST person. If both victim and accused are SC/ST members, the POA Act does not apply ‚Äî the case is registered under ordinary criminal law instead.
Q3Which section of the POA Act lists the main atrocity offences?
Section 3 of the POA Act lists all the specific offences that constitute "atrocities" ‚Äî from forcing inedible substances to social boycott, sexual violence, and destruction of property. Section 4 deals with public servant neglect, and Section 14 establishes Special Courts.
Q4What did Section 18A (inserted in 2018) change about the FIR process in atrocity cases?
Section 18A (2018) was passed to overrule the Supreme Court's Subhash Kashinath judgment which had introduced preliminary inquiry and prior approval requirements. Section 18A restores the original intent: FIR must be registered immediately, and arrest does not require prior approval from any authority.
Q5Under Rule 12 of the SC/ST (POA) Rules, the first instalment of compensation must be paid:
Rule 12 prescribes mandatory interim compensation regardless of conviction. At least 25% of the total prescribed compensation must be paid within 3-7 days of the incident (or within 3-7 days of application to the District Magistrate). Compensation is a victim's right, not charity.
Q6What is a PLV's primary role when an atrocity victim approaches them?
A PLV cannot appear in court (only empanelled advocates can). The PLV's role is field-based: listen and believe the victim, document the incident accurately, accompany them to the police station, ensure the FIR is registered under the POA Act, and connect them with DLSA for legal aid and Rule 12 compensation.
Q7What does Section 15A of the POA Act guarantee to victims?
Section 15A (inserted in 2015) is the victim rights charter of the POA Act. It guarantees victims the right to be heard when the accused applies for bail or parole, the right to be informed of case progress, the right to free legal aid from DLSA, and the right to timely payment of compensation.
Q8Which official is responsible for disbursing Rule 12 interim relief to atrocity victims?
The District Magistrate (DM) / Collector is the key authority for Rule 12 interim relief disbursement. The DM chairs the DVMC and is responsible for ensuring victims receive compensation within the prescribed timelines. A PLV should approach the DM's office if relief is not paid within 3-7 days.
Q9Which court has the exclusive power to try atrocity offences under the POA Act?
Section 14 of the POA Act requires the State Government to designate Special Courts for the exclusive trial of atrocity offences. These courts are presided over by a judge not below the rank of a Sessions Judge. Cases cannot be tried by ordinary criminal courts.
Q10A landlord (from a general/upper caste) forces an SC agricultural worker to clean a sewer without pay, abusing them with caste slurs. Which POA Act sections are potentially attracted?
Section 3(1)(f) covers forced or bonded labour imposed on an SC/ST person. Section 3(1)(u) covers intentional insult or intimidation with intent to humiliate, including caste-based verbal abuse. Both apply here. Additionally, if the sewer work is part of a hazardous cleaning assignment, the Manual Scavenging Act may also apply. A PLV should help the victim file an FIR citing all applicable sections.
