Level 1: Understanding the Act

PCR Act 1955, POA Act 1989, key definitions, 38+ scheduled offences, who can file and mandatory reporting duties

Constitutional Foundation

Article 17 of the Constitution abolishes "untouchability" and makes its practice a punishable offence. This is the bedrock on which all SC/ST protection legislation rests.

Article 341 & 342 empower the President to specify Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes respectively. Only those on the Presidential List enjoy protection under the POA Act.

Key Point: A person must belong to an SC or ST as notified by the President for their State/UT to claim protection under the SC/ST (PoA) Act 1989. Being an OBC or belonging to a non-notified community does not attract this Act.

Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955

The PCR Act penalises enforcement of disabilities arising from untouchability. It deals with access to places and services, not physical violence.

Key Offences under PCR Act

Sec 3
Preventing access to public worship places, tanks, wells, bathing ghats, roads, and other places of public resort
Sec 4
Enforcing social disabilities — refusing to sell goods, refusing service in hospitals, refusing admission to schools
Sec 5
Refusing to admit persons to hospitals, dispensaries, educational institutions
Sec 7A
Compelling persons to practice scavenging, flaying, or other customary occupations based on caste
Punishment: PCR Act — imprisonment up to 6 months and/or fine up to ₹500 (enhanced for subsequent offences). Less severe than POA Act, but covers civil disabilities that may not qualify as "atrocities."

SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989

The POA Act addresses physical, social, economic and psychological atrocities committed against SC/ST persons by non-SC/ST persons. It is far more powerful than the PCR Act.

Critical Definitions

TermMeaning
AtrocityAny offence under Sec 3(1) or 3(2) of the POA Act
Non-SC/ST perpetratorOffence must be committed by a person who is NOT an SC or ST — inter-community aspect is essential
VictimSC/ST person against whom an atrocity offence is committed or attempted
DependentRelatives dependent on victim for maintenance — entitled to relief
Public servantPolice, magistrates, revenue officials who wilfully neglect duties under the Act
Critical Rule: If both accused and victim belong to SC/ST, the POA Act does not apply. The offence must be perpetrated by a non-SC/ST person against an SC/ST person.

Who Can File a Complaint?

Sec 9 Any SC/ST victim or their relative can file. There is NO requirement to first approach police — a complaint can be made directly to a Magistrate.

Mandatory Reporting Duty

Sec 4 — Wilful Neglect: Any public servant who willfully neglects his duties under the Act is punishable with imprisonment up to 1 year. This includes police officers who refuse to register FIRs, and revenue/administrative officials who fail to provide relief.

As a field worker, your role includes:

  • Supporting victim in writing complaint
  • Accompanying victim to police station
  • Documenting refusal to register FIR (create a written record)
  • Escalating to SP/DSP if station-level police refuse
  • Informing DLSA for free legal aid (Sec 12 LSA Act)

📝 Level 1 Assessment — 20 Questions (Pass: 70%)

Q1. Which Constitutional Article absolutely abolishes untouchability?
Art. 17 abolishes untouchability and makes its practice an offence punishable by law — the only Fundamental Right that applies directly against private persons.
Q2. The PCR Act 1955 primarily deals with which type of offences?
PCR Act targets enforcement of civil disabilities — denial of access to wells, temples, shops, schools — based on untouchability practice.
Q3. For the POA Act to apply, the accused must be:
The inter-community element is essential — the offence must be committed by a non-SC/ST person against an SC/ST person. If both parties belong to SC/ST, the POA Act does not apply.
Q4. Which section deals with dumping excreta or carcasses at an SC/ST person's home?
Sec 3(1)(ii) covers dumping excreta, waste, carcasses, or objects of a fouling nature in the premises or neighborhood of an SC/ST person.
Q5. The 2016 Amendment added how many new offences to the POA Act?
The SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Amendment Act 2016 added 19 new offences to the original 22, bringing the total to 41+ scheduled offences.
Q6. Garlanding with footwear or parading someone naked is covered under:
Sec 3(1)(s) — inserted by 2016 Amendment — specifically covers garlanding with footwear and parading naked or semi-naked as atrocity offences.
Q7. Under Sec 4 POA Act, a public servant who willfully neglects duties is punishable with imprisonment up to:
Sec 4 prescribes punishment of up to 1 year's imprisonment for public servants who willfully neglect their duties under the Act — this is a powerful tool against uncooperative police.
Q8. Which Article empowers the President to specify Scheduled Castes?
Art. 341 — Scheduled Castes; Art. 342 — Scheduled Tribes. Both require Presidential notification, which is community-specific and State/UT-specific.
Q9. Wrongful dispossession from land allotted by government to SC/ST falls under:
Sec 3(1)(t) — added by 2016 Amendment — covers wrongful occupation or cultivation of land allotted, or notified, to be allotted to SC/ST members or acquired by them.
Q10. A complaint under the POA Act can be filed directly to a:
Under Sec 9, complaints can be made to any police officer or to a Magistrate. There is no requirement to first approach the police — the Magistrate route bypasses uncooperative police.
Q11. False/malicious prosecution against an SC/ST person is covered under:
Sec 3(1)(xi) covers false or malicious prosecution before a court or public servant — a common tactic used to harass SC/ST persons who raise voices against atrocities.
Q12. Forcing bonded labour on SC/ST persons falls under which section?
Sec 3(1)(iii) covers compelling SC/ST persons to do begar or other forms of forced or bonded labour. Sec 3(2)(vi) covers compelling through dominant position.
Q13. Rape or gang rape of an SC/ST woman by a non-SC/ST man is punishable under:
Sec 3(2)(v) specifically covers rape and gang rape of SC/ST women — minimum 6 years imprisonment. This is ADDITIONAL to IPC provisions and provides enhanced punishment.
Q14. Arson — setting fire to the house or property of an SC/ST — falls under:
Sec 3(2)(iii) covers arson — deliberately setting fire to any house, goods, vehicles, crops, or other property belonging to SC/ST persons.
Q15. Which section prevents SC/ST persons from using common property resources like burial grounds?
Sec 3(1)(u) — added by 2016 Amendment — covers denial of rights over common property resources including burial grounds, pasture land, and water sources.
Q16. A public humiliation or abusive insult in public based on SC/ST identity is covered by:
Sec 3(1)(x) covers intentional insult/intimidation in public; Sec 3(1)(r) — added 2016 — covers intentional public humiliation specifically targeting caste identity. Both may apply together.
Q17. Polluting a water source used by SC/ST persons falls under:
Sec 3(1)(viii) covers polluting water sources — wells, springs, ponds — or making them unfit for use that SC/ST communities habitually use.
Q18. If a police officer refuses to register an FIR for an atrocity offence, the appropriate escalation is to the:
The immediate superior of the station officer is the DSP/SP. A written complaint to SP attracts their supervisory duty. The Magistrate route is also available for direct FIR-equivalent complaints.
Q19. Compelling forcing of caste-based occupation (e.g., scavenging, flaying) is covered under PCR Act:
PCR Act Sec 7A specifically penalises compelling people to do scavenging, flaying, removing carcasses or similar customary occupations tied to untouchability.
Q20. A field worker accompanying a victim to the police station should document police refusal to register FIR by:
A written record of refusal, with witness signatures, is essential evidence. Immediate escalation to SP/DSP and simultaneous notification to DLSA for legal aid is the correct protocol.
