ЁЯУЬ Module 1: Statutory Foundations

~60 min тЦ╛

A granular reading of the SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, 1989 ("POA Act"), the Protection of Civil Rights Act, 1955 ("PCR Act"), and the 2015/2018 amendments тАФ essential before any appearance in a Special Court or High Court.

Legislative History & Constitutional Basis

Constitutional Mandate

Arts. 17 (abolition of untouchability), 21 (right to life & dignity), 46 (DPSP тАФ protection of weaker sections), and the enabling power under Art. 35 read with 15(4) & 16(4) form the bedrock of this legislation. The SC/ST Act is not penal legislation alone тАФ it is a constitutional instrument of social transformation. Courts have consistently refused to read it narrowly: State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale (1995) 4 SCC 469.

Timeline of Enactments

  • 1955 тАФ Protection of Civil Rights Act (formerly Untouchability (Offences) Act) тАФ deals with practice of untouchability-based disabilities
  • 1989 тАФ SC/ST (Prevention of Atrocities) Act, No. 33 of 1989; Rules notified 1995
  • 2015 тАФ SC/ST (POA) Amendment Act; substantial expansion of offences, addition of Sections 3(2)(v-a) to (vii), new Chapters on Rights of Victims & Witnesses
  • 2016 тАФ Amendment Rules adding Schedule of socioeconomic indicators and additional compensation heads
  • 2018 тАФ SC/ST (POA) Amendment Act; restoration of automatic arrest provision and removal of preliminary inquiry requirement after Subhash Kashinath Mahajan dilution

TermDefinition / Significance

Critical Identification Issue

The caste identity of the victim is a sine qua non of prosecution. The accused need not have known the victim's caste at the time of the offence тАФ knowledge of caste is NOT an element of the offence for most s.3 sub-clauses. See Masumsha Hasanasha Musalman v. State of Maharashtra (2000) 3 SCC 557.

Section 4 тАФ Wilful Neglect by Public Servants

Mandatory Duty & Prosecution

S.4 creates criminal liability for a public servant (not belonging to SC/ST) who, knowing an offence u/s.3 has been committed, willfully neglects duties under the Act. Post-2015 Amendment: S.4(2) mandates prosecution of any public servant who commits any s.3 offence while on duty. The 2015 Amendment inserted specific timelines тАФ investigation must be completed within 60 days, non-compliance is a s.4 violation. Under Rule 7 of the 1995 Rules, investigation by a police officer below DSP rank is itself prosecutable under s.4.

ЁЯПЫя╕П Module 2: Special Courts & Jurisdiction

~50 min тЦ╛

Jurisdiction under the Act is conferred exclusively on Special Courts and Exclusive Special Courts. Understanding these provisions is critical for both prosecution and defence, and for High Court challenges.

Jurisdiction тАФ Key Rule

The Special Court has exclusive jurisdiction for offences under s.3 and s.4 of the Act. If the accused is also charged under IPC, the Special Court can try those offences too (s.14). Committal to Sessions is not required тАФ Special Court cognizance is direct. See Gangula Ashok v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2000) 2 SCC 504.

Section 15A тАФ Rights of Victims & Witnesses [2015 Amendment]

Statutory Rights of Victims

  • Right to be heard at every stage тАФ bail, suspension of sentence, appeal
  • Right to be informed of the case progress, release/bail of accused
  • Right to reasonable, accurate, and timely notice of any public court proceedings
  • Right to be present and reasonably heard at all public proceedings
  • Right to be treated with fairness, respect, and dignity
  • Right to a speedy trial (s.15A(4) тАФ State's obligation)
  • Right to medical expenses, rehabilitation, and compensation (s.17)

Witness Protection

  • S.15A(3): Witnesses are entitled to protection from the State at all stages of investigation and trial
  • The Special Court may direct in-camera proceedings, identity protection, video testimony
  • Duty on Special Public Prosecutor and Investigating Officer to ensure witness safety
  • Harassment of witnesses by accused or their associates is a s.3(1)(h) offence

Section 14A тАФ Appeals

Direct HC Appeal тАФ No Sessions Appeal

S.14A(1): Notwithstanding the CrPC, an appeal against any judgment, sentence, or order of a Special Court shall lie exclusively to the High Court. Bail orders by Special Court are also directly challengeable before HC. The HC is mandated to decide s.14A appeals within 3 months. This bars Sessions Court from entertaining any revision/appeal тАФ a procedural point frequently raised in practice.

Common Error

Filing a revision/appeal before the Sessions Court against a Special Court order is without jurisdiction. The only remedy is HC under s.14A. Ensure you check this before filing тАФ contempt and costs possible for forum shopping.

Section 16 тАФ Collective Fines

Power of State Government

Where the State Government is satisfied after enquiry that persons residing in an area have connived at or abetted the commission of an atrocity, or have failed to prevent the commission, the State may impose a collective fine on those persons. This provision is rarely invoked but available in cases of mass mob atrocities where individual prosecution is difficult.

ЁЯФН Module 3: Prosecutorial Obligations & Defence Strategies

~60 min тЦ╛

The Act creates specific obligations on the prosecution machinery. Understanding these тАФ both for appearing as Special Public Prosecutor and for challenging prosecution as defence counsel тАФ is essential lawyering under the Act.

Section 15 тАФ Special Public Prosecutors

Appointment & Qualifications

  • S.15: State Government shall appoint a Special Public Prosecutor for every Special Court
  • Qualifications: Advocate with at least 7 years' practice at Bar OR retired judicial/IPS officer
  • 2015 Amendment: Empanelment may include advocates recommended by SC/ST organisations
  • The SPP is distinct from the regular PP/APP тАФ a non-SPP cannot conduct SC/ST Act trials
Trial by Non-Designated Prosecutor

If the trial is conducted by a PP/APP who has not been designated as SPP, the entire trial is vitiated тАФ this is a ground for conviction being set aside on appeal. Always verify appointment of SPP at the outset. See State of Madhya Pradesh v. Ram Krishna Balothia (1995) 3 SCC 221.

Anticipatory Bail тАФ Section 18 & 18A

The Anticipatory Bail Bar

S.18: "Nothing in section 438 of the CrPC shall apply in relation to any case involving the arrest of any person on an accusation of having committed an offence under this Act." This is an absolute bar. S.18A [2018 Amendment] further provides that no preliminary inquiry shall be conducted, and the officer shall arrest the accused forthwith. The Supreme Court in Prithvi Raj Chauhan v. UOI (2020) 4 SCC 727 upheld ss.18 & 18A тАФ anticipatory bail remains barred. Even a High Court cannot grant anticipatory bail u/s.438 where the accusation is under the POA Act.

Exception тАФ Manifestly False Cases

The constitutional courts (HC and SC) retain inherent powers under Art. 226/32 and CrPC s.482 where the complaint is patently false and lodged mala fide. However, the threshold is very high. A mere allegation of false complaint is insufficient тАФ the court must be satisfied that no prima facie offence is made out. See Union of India v. Ramesh Ram (2010) 7 SCC 234.

Section 21 тАФ Presumption as to Offences

Rebuttable Presumption in Favour of Victim

Where the victim states that the accused did commit an act constituting an offence under the Act, the court shall presume such an offence was committed, unless the contrary is proved. This presumption (similar to s.113A/B IEA) reverses the evidential burden in certain cases. The prosecution must still prove the caste identity of victim and accused, and that the act was committed тАФ whereupon the POA Act presumption kicks in for the "atrocity" element.

ЁЯУЛ Module 4: PCR Act 1955 & Interaction with IPC

The PCR Act is a distinct statute targeting the practice of untouchability. It operates alongside тАФ and is sometimes charged with тАФ the POA Act. The interaction with IPC offences requires careful handling to avoid double jeopardy concerns and to maximise protection.

Charging Strategy тАФ PCR Act + POA Act + IPC

Typical Multi-Charge Framework

  • PCR Act s.4 (enforcing social disability) + POA Act s.3(1)(о') (denial of right of way) + IPC s.341 (wrongful restraint) тАФ for denial of access to public places
  • PCR Act s.3 + POA Act s.3(1)(j) (insult in public view) тАФ for temple entry cases
  • POA Act s.3(2)(v) + IPC s.376 + POCSO (if minor) тАФ for sexual offences against SC/ST women/girls
  • POA Act s.3(1)(d)/(e) + IPC s.448 (trespass) тАФ for land dispossession
Double Jeopardy

Charging under both PCR Act and POA Act for the exact same act may invite an Art. 20(2)/s.300 CrPC plea. The safe practice is to charge the same conduct under both Acts only where the ingredients differ тАФ PCR Act requires "enforcement of untouchability" (identity/social disability nexus), while POA Act ss.3(1)(j)/(r) may be broader. Always articulate the distinct factual basis foreach charge.

Compounding & Withdrawal

Non-Compoundable Status

Offences under the POA Act are not compoundable тАФ neither under the First Schedule to CrPC nor by judicial order. The Special Court cannot permit compounding. The prosecution can only be withdrawn u/s.321 CrPC by the SPP with leave of court тАФ and even this is permissible only in exceptional circumstances and subject to the court's satisfaction that it is in public interest. A politically motivated withdrawal can be challenged by the victim u/s.15A or by filing a fresh private complaint/writ petition.
