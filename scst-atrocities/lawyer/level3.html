📚 Module 1: Landmark Supreme Court Judgments

~70 min ▾

The SC/ST Act's journey through the Supreme Court has been marked by tension between the Act's protective purpose and concerns about misuse. Every practitioner must know this arc — from the Act's broad application to the Subhash Kashinath controversy and its reversal by Parliament.

Foundational Cases — Scope & Interpretation

State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale
(1995) Supp(4) SCC 469
Ratio: SC/ST Act is a social legislation — must be construed broadly

The Supreme Court held that the SC/ST Act, being a welfare and protection legislation, must be interpreted liberally in favour of the victims. Courts must not import technical limitations from general criminal law when the clear purpose is social upliftment. Technical objections that dilute the Act's protective sweep should be disfavoured. This principle continues to guide statutory interpretation of the Act.

📌 Application: Use this ratio when opposing technical defences based on procedural objections or narrow reading of s.3 sub-sections.
Masumsha Hasanasha Musalman v. State of Maharashtra
(2000) 3 SCC 557
Ratio: Accused's knowledge of victim's caste is NOT required for most s.3 offences

The Court definitively held that for offences under s.3, it is not necessary that the accused knew the victim belonged to a Scheduled Caste or Scheduled Tribe at the time of the commission of the offence. The victim's SC/ST status and the commission of the prohibited act are sufficient. The "caste motivation" requirement is specific to certain sub-sections — it is not a general ingredient. This was a critical ruling against defence counsel who routinely argued lack of knowledge.

📌 Application: In any case where defence argues "accused didn't know victim's caste", cite this case.
Gangula Ashok v. State of Andhra Pradesh
(2000) 2 SCC 504
Ratio: Special Court has exclusive jurisdiction — Magistrate cannot try SC/ST Act offences

The Supreme Court held that the Special Court has exclusive jurisdiction to try SC/ST Act offences. If a Magistrate takes cognizance of such an offence, the proceedings are without jurisdiction and must be transferred to the Special Court. This ruling is fundamental to jurisdictional challenges at the inception of proceedings.

📌 Application: Where case is before wrong court, apply for transfer to Special Court without delay — any delay may be argued as waiver of this objection by prosecution.
State of Maharashtra v. Ram Krishna Balothia
(1995) 3 SCC 221
Ratio: SC/ST Act is constitutionally valid — bar on anticipatory bail is not ultra vires Art. 21

This was among the earliest constitutional challenges to the Act. The Court upheld the validity of the absolute bar on anticipatory bail (s.18) and held that it does not violate Art. 21 (right to life and personal liberty). The State's compelling interest in protecting historically oppressed communities justifies the differential treatment. Art. 15(4) and 46 (DPSPs) must be read with Art. 21 — they form a constitutional continuum.

📌 Application: When constitutional validity of s.18 is challenged before HC, cite this along with Prithvi Raj Chauhan (2020).

The Subhash Kashinath Controversy & Legislative Response

Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra
(2018) 6 SCC 454 — Two-Judge Bench
Controversial Direction — Overturned by 2018 Amendment

A two-judge bench of the Supreme Court — without referral to a larger bench — issued directions that: (1) required a preliminary inquiry before registration of FIR, (2) made arrest subject to prior approval of DSP/SSP, (3) granted anticipatory bail in "exceptional cases". These directions were criticised as judicially diluting the protections Parliament had enacted. Unprecedented nationwide protests by Dalit organisations followed.

📌 Status: Parliament responded with the SC/ST (POA) Amendment Act 2018, inserting s.18A to expressly bar preliminary inquiry and restore mandatory arrest. The 2018 judgment's directions are now superseded by statute.
Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (Review)
(2019) — Review Petition
Ratio: The Court recalls and reviews some of its earlier directions

In the review petition, the Supreme Court itself recalled certain directions from its 2018 judgment — particularly the preliminary inquiry requirement — acknowledging the legislative intent and the 2018 Amendment. This partial recall, combined with the statutory amendment, renders the original Subhash Kashinath directions wholly inoperative. However, the judgment remains cited by those seeking quashing of FIRs — every practitioner must be ready to distinguish it.

📌 Application: When defence cites Subhash Kashinath to argue for pre-arrest bail or preliminary inquiry, counter with: (1) the review recall, (2) the 2018 Amendment (s.18A), and (3) Prithvi Raj Chauhan (2020).
Prithvi Raj Chauhan v. Union of India
(2020) 4 SCC 727 — Constitution Bench (5 Judges)
Ratio: The 2018 Amendment is constitutionally valid; ss.18 and 18A are upheld

The Constitution Bench conclusively upheld the SC/ST (POA) Amendment Act 2018. The Court held: (i) the absolute bar on anticipatory bail u/s.18 is valid, (ii) s.18A's prohibition on preliminary inquiry is valid, (iii) the Subhash Kashinath Mahajan (2018) directions are no longer operative. The five-judge bench overruled the earlier two-judge bench to the extent of inconsistency. This is now the settled law on the subject.

📌 This is the most important recent judgment under the Act — cite it in every bail and quashing matter to shut down Subhash Kashinath-based arguments.

⚔️ Module 2: High Court & Supreme Court Litigation Strategy

~60 min ▾

SC/ST Act litigation frequently reaches the High Court — for quashing, s.14A appeals, bail appeals, and writ petitions. Knowing the strategic framework for each forum is essential for effective representation.

Quashing — CrPC s.482 / Art. 226 HC Petition

Grounds for Quashing FIR (Defence)

  • No cognizable offence disclosed — the act as described does not fall within any s.3 sub-section
  • The accused belongs to SC/ST — the foundational inter-community element is absent
  • The victim does not belong to SC/ST community — misidentification
  • The act occurred in a non-public setting where s.3(1)(j) (public view) is charged
  • FIR is a counter-blast to a civil dispute / clear mala fides evidenced from simultaneous civil proceedings
  • Complaint filed after inordinate delay with suspicious circumstances suggesting false case
The Prima Facie Test — Not a Mini-Trial

In quashing proceedings under s.482 CrPC / Art. 226, the HC examines only whether the FIR on its face discloses an offence. The HC does not conduct a trial, evaluate evidence, or assess credibility of witnesses. Unless the case falls squarely within the Bhajan Lal categories, the FIR will not be quashed. The SC in Ramesh Kumar v. State of Chhattisgarh (2001) 9 SCC 618 cautioned against routine quashing of SC/ST Act FIRs — it defeats the legislative purpose.

Victim's Right to Oppose Quashing

Under s.15A, victims have a right to be heard at every stage. Victims (or their counsel) should file a caveat before the HC and oppose quashing petitions. The victim/complainant has standing to appear and oppose — even if the State does not oppose vigorously. Use s.15A(5)(b): "The State shall ensure that the victim is informed of all public court proceedings."

PIL Strategy — Systemic Violations

Categories of Systemic PILs

  • Non-appointment of Special Courts / SPPs: Where states have not designated Exclusive Special Courts or appointed SPPs as mandated by s.14/s.15 — writ of mandamus to the State Government
  • Non-payment of compensation: Where state governments are systematically failing to pay s.17 compensation — writ compelling payment with interest
  • Non-investigation / closure reports: Where police file closure reports in SC/ST Act cases without proper investigation — writ directing fresh investigation by SIT
  • Mass atrocities: Village boycotts, burning of Dalit homes — writ for protection, interim stay of perpetrators' movement, police protection order
  • Caste discrimination in government institutions: Structural atrocities in prisons, hospitals, schools — s.3(1)(r)/(j) violations by public servants
National Campaign on Dalit Human Rights v. Union of India
W.P.(C) 1015/2008 — Supreme Court
Writ for enforcement of SC/ST Act implementation

This PIL challenged the systemic failure of state governments to implement the SC/ST Act — non-appointment of Exclusive Special Courts, non-payment of compensation, non-investigation of atrocities. The SC issued detailed directions for compliance monitoring, including quarterly reports to HCs. This case established the template for systemic enforcement PILs under the Act.

📌 Use this as precedent for any PIL challenging state non-compliance with the Act's institutional mandates.

💰 Module 3: Compensation Enforcement & Contempt

~50 min ▾

Securing compensation under the Act is both a statutory right and a constitutional entitlement. Enforcing it requires combining s.17 proceedings, writ petitions, and contempt applications where State authorities default.

Compensation Rights — The Complete Picture

Multi-Layer Compensation Framework
  • Administrative (s.17 + Rules): Interim relief within 7 days; full relief within 60 days — from District/State government funds earmarked under s.17
  • Special Court (conviction stage): Additional compensation awarded by Special Court on conviction — not a reduction/credit against admin compensation
  • CrPC s.357A: State Victim Compensation Fund (SCVCS/SVCS scheme) — concurrent remedy; apply even before conviction
  • CrPC s.357: Court-awarded compensation from fine/property of convicted accused
  • Constitutional (Art. 21/32/226): Rudul Sah/Nilabati Behera principle — where state action caused the harm, constitutional compensation from the state lies

Contempt Proceedings for Non-Payment

When to File Contempt

  • When HC has issued mandamus to pay compensation and the state has not complied within stipulated time
  • When the DM/SDM fails th pass the s.17 interim compensation order within 7 days despite FIR registration — file contempt if there is a prior HC order/direction
  • When Special Court has awarded compensation on conviction \— art remains unpaid

Case Study — Enforcing Compensation Post-Acquittal

The Problem: Can admin compensation be recovered on acquittal?

Nronsacs governments sometem recover s.17 administrative compensation if the accused is ultimately acquitted. The courts have consistently rejected this: the administrative compensation under s.17 and the Rules is intended to provide immediate relief independent of the mUtcome of the criminal trial. Acquittal of the accused does not entitle the State to recover the compensation from the victim. Only if it is established that the victim filed a false complaint may the question arise — and even then, it is the complainant (if convicted for false complaint) who bears the consequences, not the recovery of s.17 relief. See Rule 12(3): "The relief should be given... irrespective of... registration of a case."

🗺️ Module 4: Strategic Litigation Toolkit

~40 min ▾

A step-by-step toolkit for common SC/ST Act litigation scenarios — from the first client interview to HC appeal. These are the playbooks every practitioner must internalise.

Playbook A — Police Refusing to Register FIR

1

Complaint to SP / DIG

Write a formal complaint identifying the specific s.3 sub-sections attracted. This creates a paper trail and may be enough to jolt action.

2

Application u/s.156(3) CrPC to JMFC

File a private complaint with a Judicial Magistrate First Class — the Magistrate can direct police to register FIR and investigate. This is faster than a writ and available in the district itself.

3

Complaint to DSP / NHRC / SHRC / NCSC

Simultaneously, file complaints with the National Commission for Scheduled Castes (NCSC) or SHRC — they have power to summon police officers and direct compliance.

4

Writ of Mandamus — HC Art. 226

File urgent writ petition with certified copy of complaint sent to police, affidavit of victim, and draft of proposed FIR. HC can direct FIR registration within 24 hours on an urgent mentioning.

5

Contempt + s.4 Prosecution

Once HC order exists, non-compliance attracts contempt. Simultaneously, file s.4 complaint against the errant SHO before Special Court for wilful neglect of duty.

Playbook B — Appearing as SPP in Special Court

Pre-Trial Checklist

  • Verify your appointment notification as SPP — original notification on file
  • Obtain all investigation documents: FIR, DSP investigation report, spot inspection, medical report, s.161 statements
  • Prepare witness list and exam-in-chief outlines for each witness
  • Secure s.65B certificates for all electronic evidence
  • Verify s.17 compensation has been ordered — if not, apply at cognizance stage
  • File s.15A application to ensure victim is informed of all proceedings
  • Identify potential hostile witnesses and plan corroborative evidence strategy

Playbook C — Defending in SC/ST Act Cases

Points to Investigate Immediately on Receiving Brief
  • Does the accused belong to SC/ST? If yes — the Act does not apply (inter-community requirement)
  • Is there any civil dispute/prior litigation between the parties? (mala fide indicator)
  • Was there a preliminary inquiry before FIR? (if so, s.18A violated — affects prosecution)
  • Who conducted the investigation? (check rank — if below DSP, Rule 7 violated)
  • Is the specific act within a s.3 sub-section? (public view requirement for s.3(1)(j), etc.)
  • Is there independent corroboration or only family/interested witnesses?

Ethics Note — Defence in SC/ST Act Cases

While every accused is entitled to representation, counsel must not suborn perjury, facilitate harassment of victim witnesses, or file vexatious applications solely to delay proceedings. The Act specifically criminalises instituting false or malicious proceedings against SC/ST persons (s.3(1)(h)) — using the defence role to further harassment of the victim can attract this provision against the instigator.
