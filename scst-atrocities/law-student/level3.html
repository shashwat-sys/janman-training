⚖ 1. Constitutional Compensation Doctrine

Beyond the SC/ST Act's statutory compensation framework, victims of atrocities — particularly where state complicity, police inaction, or public servant atrocity is involved — can access constitutional compensation remedies under Art.32 and Art.226. These remedies are grounded in landmark Supreme Court decisions on state liability for fundamental rights violations.

Rudul Shah v. State of Bihar
1983 — Supreme Court of India — Constitutional Compensation Foundation
The Supreme Court awarded monetary compensation for illegal detention beyond the period of sentence in an Art.32 petition — the first time the Court used its constitutional jurisdiction to award compensation for fundamental rights violations without a statutory basis. Held: when a person's fundamental rights are violated by the State, the Supreme Court under Art.32 can award compensation as part of the constitutional remedy. The reasoning: Art.32 gives the right to move the Supreme Court for enforcement of fundamental rights — and the right would be illusory if the only remedy were release without any monetary relief for the wrong suffered. This case established the doctrine of constitutional tort, which has since been extended to all categories of State violations of fundamental rights, including atrocities enabled by State action or inaction.
Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa
1993 — Supreme Court of India — Strict Liability of State
Distinguished constitutional compensation from private tort law. The son of the petitioner died in police custody. The Court held: (i) constitutional compensation is a public law remedy — distinct from private law tort action; (ii) the State cannot plead defences available in private tort (contributory negligence, volenti non fit injuria) in a constitutional compensation claim; (iii) the standard of liability for State actors violating fundamental rights is strict — it is not necessary to prove malice or intent; (iv) the compensation is awarded as "solace" for the constitutional wrong, not merely as damages for actual loss. Application to SC/ST Act: Where a public servant (police officer, revenue official) commits or enables an atrocity, the State is strictly liable for constitutional compensation under Art.32/226. The Nilabati Behera standard should be combined with §3(2)(vi) (public servant atrocity) for maximum relief.

Application to SC/ST Act Cases — When to Use Constitutional Compensation

Constitutional compensation petition under Art.32/226 is appropriate where: (1) Police inaction enabled the atrocity — police were informed but did not act; (2) A public servant committed the atrocity (§3(2)(vi) cases); (3) Atrocity occurred in custody — person taken to police station and then atrocity occurred; (4) State failed to protect SC/ST victim despite knowledge of threat; (5) Systemic failure — DVMC not meeting, compensation not paid, Special Court not functioning. Combine constitutional petition with statutory claim for maximum relief. The constitutional petition can be filed in High Court (Art.226) or Supreme Court (Art.32) and can include directions for CBI investigation, CBI monitoring, compensation, and systemic reforms.

DK Basu v. State of West Bengal
1997 — Supreme Court of India
Laid down guidelines for arrest and detention that are constitutionally mandatory. Held that custodial violence — torture, injury, or death in police custody — violates Art.21 and Art.17 where the victim is SC/ST. Compensation awarded as constitutional remedy. The "DK Basu guidelines" (now codified in CrPC/BNSS) require specific procedures for arrest, documentation, and medical examination. In SC/ST Act cases where an SC/ST person is illegally arrested and subject to custodial violence, both §4 (public servant neglect of duty) and DK Basu-based constitutional compensation are available simultaneously. Always check whether an SC/ST accused (i.e., an SC/ST person falsely implicated under §3(1)(viii)) has been subjected to custodial violence.
Quantum of Constitutional Compensation: Courts award constitutional compensation based on the gravity of the fundamental rights violation, not merely actual loss. For deaths in custody: Rs. 5–20 lakhs (varying by court and case facts). For custodial torture: Rs. 1–5 lakhs. For prolonged illegal detention: Rs. 1–3 lakhs per year. For atrocity enabling by police inaction: courts have awarded Rs. 2–10 lakhs. File an affidavit detailing all aspects of the violation — physical, psychological, economic, and social impact — to maximise quantum.

📋 2. Five More Landmark Cases — Deep Analysis

These five cases, combined with the cases from Levels 1 and 2, form the core case law library for SC/ST Act practice. Each contributes a distinct doctrinal principle that is regularly argued in Special Courts and High Courts.

State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale
1993 — Supreme Court of India
Facts: SC/ST persons were denied the right to draw water from a common well in their village by upper-caste accused — a classic untouchability practice. The Karnataka High Court reversed the conviction, holding that the evidence was insufficient. The Supreme Court restored the conviction. Ratio: Art.17 is directly enforceable — it creates an enforceable right against private individuals, not just the State. Social exclusion from public resources — water, roads, cremation grounds — on the basis of caste identity is always an "atrocity" within the SC/ST Act. The concept of untouchability extends beyond direct physical contact to any denial of common civic rights on the basis of caste. For lawyers: Use Appa Balu Ingale for any case involving denial of access to public resources — wells, panchayat buildings, schools, roads, market access. The denial of access itself, based on caste identity, is sufficient for conviction. No additional physical violence is required.
Patan Jamal Vali v. State of Andhra Pradesh
2021 — Supreme Court of India
Facts: A blind SC woman was raped. The case raised questions about how courts should approach sentencing and relief where the victim experiences multiple forms of marginalisation simultaneously. Ratio: The Court recognised and applied the doctrine of "intersectionality" — the concept that multiple axes of marginalisation (caste + disability + gender) compound each other's effects and create a qualitatively distinct and more severe form of vulnerability. The Court held that courts must recognise this compounding effect when assessing: (i) the nature and gravity of the harm suffered; (ii) appropriate sentence; (iii) quantum of compensation; (iv) systemic relief required. The Court applied "transformative constitutionalism" — interpreting the SC/ST Act not as a narrow penal statute but as a vehicle for the Constitution's project of social transformation. For lawyers: Cite Patan Jamal Vali whenever your client is at multiple intersections of marginalisation — SC/ST + woman, SC/ST + disability, SC/ST + minor, SC/ST + poverty. The intersectional analysis should strengthen your arguments on sentencing and compensation quantum.
Gurinder Singh Aujla v. State of Punjab
2020 — Supreme Court of India
Facts: An SC/ST Act case where the investigation was conducted by a police officer below the rank of DSP, contrary to §9 of the Act. The question was whether this rendered the investigation and the resulting charge sheet invalid. Ratio: Section 9's requirement that a DSP or higher-ranked officer shall investigate SC/ST Act cases is mandatory, not directory. Investigation by a sub-DSP rank officer is procedurally invalid and can be challenged before the Special Court. The Court directed fresh investigation by a DSP-rank officer. For lawyers: At the very first opportunity in every SC/ST Act case, check the rank of the IO listed on the FIR. If "Sub-Inspector" or "Inspector" is listed, immediately file an application in the Special Court challenging the investigation and seeking fresh DSP investigation. This challenge becomes stronger if the existing investigation is one-sided or has omitted key witnesses — combine the §9 violation with the substantive defects of the investigation.
NALSA v. Union of India
2014 — Supreme Court of India
Facts: While primarily a transgender rights case, the Court's analysis of structural discrimination has direct application to caste-based exclusion in SC/ST Act practice. Ratio: The Court held that historically marginalised identities have a right to dignity, recognition, and participation in public life. The State has a positive obligation to ensure that marginalised identities can access justice, social welfare, and civic rights without discrimination. The Court applied Art.21 (right to life with dignity) and Art.14 (equality) to require affirmative state action. Application to SC/ST Act: NALSA's framework supports: (i) the argument that SC/ST identity entails a right to live with dignity free from caste-based humiliation; (ii) state's positive obligation to prevent atrocities — not merely to react after they occur; (iii) the argument that caste exclusion violates Art.14 and Art.21 simultaneously, supporting constitutional compensation claims alongside statutory remedies. The intersectionality analysis from NALSA also strengthens Patan Jamal Vali arguments in cases involving gender + caste intersections.
Swaran Singh v. State of Punjab
2008 — Supreme Court of India
Facts: A SC/ST Act case where the trial court had imposed a sentence below the mandatory minimum. The question was whether the SC/ST Act's minimum sentence provisions are binding, and what principles govern sentencing. Ratio: The Court laid down guidelines for sentencing in SC/ST Act cases: (i) where the Act prescribes a minimum sentence, courts are bound by it — they cannot impose a sentence below the minimum without specific statutory authority; (ii) courts must consider the social background of the case — the entrenched caste hierarchy, the vulnerability of the victim, the power disparity between accused and victim; (iii) courts must factor in the deterrence principle — SC/ST Act sentences should deter caste violence; (iv) maximum sentence is appropriate in cases of aggravated offences involving rape, arson, custodial atrocity. For lawyers: Cite Swaran Singh at the sentencing stage in every SC/ST Act case. Prepare a sentencing note addressing: social background, vulnerability of victim, deterrence, impact on the victim's family and community. Oppose any attempt by the defence to seek minimum sentence in aggravated offence cases.

🔍 3. Writ Strategy in SC/ST Act Cases

Writ petitions under Art.226 (High Court) and Art.32 (Supreme Court) are powerful tools for SC/ST Act victims when the ordinary statutory machinery fails. Each writ remedy targets a specific failure in the system — know which writ to use in which situation.

Writ of Mandamus

Compelling FIR Registration and Investigation

  • When: Police refuses FIR despite written complaint to SP and §156(3) order from Magistrate
  • Grounds: Statutory duty under §154 CrPC + §18A SC/ST Act + Lalita Kumari mandate — no preliminary inquiry permitted
  • Relief sought: Direction to SHO/SP to register FIR within 24/48 hours and report compliance; direction for DSP-level investigation under §9
  • Pre-requisites: Written complaint to SP, §156(3) attempt, both failures documented with dates and acknowledgements
  • Contempt protection: Non-compliance with mandamus order = contempt of court — powerful enforcement mechanism
  • Also use for: Compelling DVMC meetings, compelling appointment of SPP, compelling designation of Special Court
Writ of Certiorari

Quashing Illegal Orders

  • To quash bail granted without hearing victim: §15A(5) right violated — any bail order without victim's hearing is jurisdictionally defective
  • To quash transfer from Special Court: Any order transferring SC/ST Act case from Special Court to regular court is void — Special Court has exclusive jurisdiction
  • To quash §9 violation: Where Special Court permits investigation by sub-DSP officer — challenge the permitting order
  • To quash anticipatory bail: Any anticipatory bail granted in SC/ST Act case (even by Sessions Court in error) — §18A makes it void
  • Grounds: Jurisdiction error + violation of statutory mandate + violation of victim's Art.21 rights
  • File immediately: Do not wait — illegal bail orders must be challenged within days of the order
Art.32 / Art.226

Constitutional Compensation Petition

  • Grounds: Police inaction enabling atrocity (Art.17 + Art.21 violation by State omission); public servant atrocity (§3(2)(vi) + Nilabati Behera strict liability)
  • State's positive obligation: Art.17 requires State to actively prevent untouchability practices — not merely to prosecute after atrocity occurs
  • Relief sought: Compensation under constitutional tort doctrine; direction for CBI/SIT investigation; systemic reforms — DVMC functioning, SP accountability
  • Combine with: PIL jurisdiction for systemic failures; direction for contempt proceedings against non-compliant officials
  • Evidence to file: Affidavit detailing all aspects of violation; RTI data on atrocity rates; NCRB data for PIL; field documentation
  • Standard: Strict liability (Nilabati Behera) — no need to prove malice, intent, or negligence of State actors
PIL Jurisdiction

Systemic Failures and Structural Remedies

  • DVMC not meeting: Rules 1995 require mandatory periodic DVMC meetings — non-compliance can be challenged by PIL
  • SPP not appointed: §15 — mandatory appointment; non-appointment challengeable; vacancies = rights denial
  • Special Courts not functional: §14 "shall" = mandatory designation; backlog data from NCRB supports PIL on fast-tracking
  • Compensation not paid: Schedule I compensation is mandatory upon conviction — systematic non-payment challengeable by PIL
  • Petitioner: Any person — lawyers, civil society organisations, affected communities, journalists — can file PIL on constitutional issues
  • Evidence base: RTI responses on atrocity data; NCRB crime statistics; NHRC/NCSC reports; field documentation by NGOs
Writ Petition Drafting Principles for SC/ST Act Cases: (1) Frame every ground constitutionally — link statutory violation to Art.17, Art.21, Art.14; (2) Cite Lalita Kumari, Prathvi Raj Chauhan, Appa Balu Ingale, Nilabati Behera as appropriate; (3) Seek interim relief in the same petition — typically an order protecting the victim, directing FIR registration, or staying the illegal bail; (4) Pray for systemic directions where appropriate — not just individual relief; (5) File affidavit of facts — sworn by the victim or victim's family member — as the core of the writ. The constitutional court will act on verified facts, not merely legal arguments.

📋 4. Trial Advocacy in Special Courts

Special Courts require a distinctive advocacy approach — combining the urgency of rights-based work with the precision of criminal trial procedure. As victim's counsel under §15A(7), or as a future Special Public Prosecutor, master these trial advocacy principles.

  • Time-bound trial — §309 CrPC/BNSS: Special Courts must proceed day-to-day and record specific reasons in writing for every adjournment. As victim's counsel, object to routine adjournments on record. File written objections citing §309 and the right to speedy trial. Routine adjournments delay justice, exhaust victim's resources, and enable witness tampering.
  • Victim counsel under §15A(7): File your vakalatnama as private advocate appearing alongside the SPP. Ensure you are served notice of all hearings. Attend all charge-framing hearings, bail hearings, and examination of witnesses. Supplement the SPP's examination with your questions — the court will permit additional questions from victim's counsel under §15A(11).
  • Examination-in-chief of survivor/victim: Lead the survivor through the narrative chronologically. Establish caste identity at the very beginning — introduce caste certificate into evidence early. Establish the caste motivation through context — the prior relationship between parties, community dynamics, any statements by accused. Build to the specific act of atrocity. Keep questions simple, non-leading, and sequential. Allow the survivor to use their own language.
  • Anticipating cross-examination by defense: Prepare the witness for: (i) "Caste not mentioned at time" — prepare: Manohar Lal; circumstantial inference; community context; (ii) "Political/property enmity" — gather evidence of prior incidents, community dynamics, power disparity; (iii) "Delay in complaint" — explain: fear, community pressure, no access to police, dependence on accused community for livelihood; (iv) "Inconsistency with §164 statement" — explain minor inconsistencies as normal memory variation, focus on core facts. Prepare rehabilitation questions for each anticipated attack.
  • Framing of charges: Under §15A(11), actively participate at charge-framing stage. Ensure all applicable §3 sub-sections are charged — prosecutors sometimes under-charge. File a written note to the SPP listing all applicable sections before the charge-framing hearing. If charges are improperly framed, file an application for additional charges or seek revision before the High Court.
  • Sentencing submissions: §3 minimum sentences are mandatory — cite Swaran Singh. Prepare a detailed sentencing note: (i) victim's social situation and vulnerability; (ii) impact of atrocity on victim's family and community; (iii) deterrence — data on atrocity rates in district; (iv) accused's position and influence in community; (v) lack of remorse. File victim impact statement as part of sentencing submissions — courts can receive this under §15A(11).
  • §357A CrPC Compensation: Always claim compensation from the State Victim Compensation Fund under §357A CrPC (§395 BNSS) at trial stage — in addition to any conviction and sentence. Also remind the court to direct interim compensation from the state government under the SC/ST Rules, 1995 compensation schedule. Never leave a trial without having addressed compensation. These are two separate claims — pursue both.

The SPP's Constitutional Duty

The Special Public Prosecutor is not merely a state-paid criminal lawyer. The SPP occupies a constitutional role — as the state's instrument for enforcing Art.17 through criminal prosecution. The SPP's duty includes: (i) ensuring victim's rights under §15A are respected throughout trial; (ii) opposing bail applications vigorously; (iii) making sentencing submissions that reflect the gravity of caste violence; (iv) filing appeals against acquittal where the facts warrant. An SPP who treats SC/ST Act cases as routine criminal matters has failed their constitutional duty. As an SPP candidate or supervising lawyer, approach every case with the gravity of a constitutional rights enforcement action.

⚖ 5. Professional Ethics — Lawyer's Duties in SC/ST Act Cases

Practice in SC/ST Act cases is not merely technical legal work. It is participation in the constitutional project of caste abolition mandated by Art.17. This demands specific ethical commitments that go beyond the general duties of advocacy.

🤝

Trauma-Informed Practice

Survivors of caste atrocities have experienced multiple layers of humiliation — often at the hands of community members they trusted, and sometimes at the hands of police and officials who were supposed to protect them. Interview in a private, safe space. Use respectful forms of address. Never express skepticism about the caste identity of the victim or the caste motivation of the accused. Never ask "why didn't you resist?" or "why didn't you report earlier?" without first understanding the community context of power and fear. Understand that silence and delay are survival strategies, not signs of dishonesty.

⚖

Duty of Honesty — No False Promises

You cannot promise acquittal, a specific sentence, or any particular outcome — this violates Bar Council of India Rules on professional conduct. Explain the legal process, timeline, evidence requirements, and realistic range of outcomes honestly. Acknowledge the challenges — hostile witnesses, corrupt police, slow courts — while explaining the legal tools available. False hope destroys trust when reality differs. Honest counselling builds the long-term engagement needed for a complex case.

📋

Documentation and Case Diary

Maintain contemporaneous notes of every client meeting — what was discussed, what instructions were given, what was advised. Maintain a case diary tracking every court date, every order, every filing. Keep copies of all correspondence — with police, court, DLSA, NCSC. Documentation protects both the client's case and your professional conduct. In SC/ST Act cases where officials routinely deny having been informed, contemporaneous notes and acknowledged correspondence are your evidence of their culpability.

🚫

Conflict of Interest

Never represent both the victim and a person accused of an SC/ST Act offence in different cases — even in different districts. The community dynamics of caste atrocity cases create structural conflicts of interest. Be vigilant about community and political pressure — in rural areas, powerful accused communities may approach victim's counsel through intermediaries offering financial settlement. All such approaches must be disclosed to the client. The SC/ST Act makes all offences non-compoundable — the victim cannot legally "settle" with the accused, and any pressure on the victim to do so may itself constitute an offence.

🌟

Transformative Purpose

The lawyer's role in SC/ST Act cases is not just legal — it is part of the constitutional project of caste abolition under Art.17. Every case won is a step toward the Constitution's transformative vision. Every victim whose §15A rights are enforced is a step toward equal citizenship. Approach this work with the gravity it deserves — not as pro bono charity, but as constitutional advocacy. Read Dr. Ambedkar. Understand the history of the Act. Connect legal strategy to constitutional purpose.

🔐

Confidentiality and Privacy

Client confidentiality applies with particular force in SC/ST Act cases — disclosure of the client's identity, case details, or evidence can endanger the victim and witnesses. Do not discuss case details publicly or with persons not directly involved. Be especially careful about social media — case details should not appear online without explicit client consent. In cases involving rape (§3(2)(v)), the victim's identity is also protected by §228A IPC/BNS — never disclose in any public forum.

The Constitutional Obligation of Caste-Conscious Practice

Many lawyers — including SC/ST lawyers themselves — have been trained in "caste-blind" legal practice. But caste-blindness in the practice of SC/ST Act law is a form of professional failure. You cannot effectively represent SC/ST victims without understanding how caste operates in the specific village or district — the social hierarchy, the power dynamics, the patterns of impunity, and the specific forms caste atrocity takes in that region. This is not prejudice — it is the contextual competence that the law itself demands. The Act was designed for lawyers who understand caste — as a historical reality, a social structure, and a constitutional target for elimination. Be that lawyer.

Continuing Professional Development: SC/ST Act practice requires ongoing learning. Sources for continued development: (1) NCRB Annual Report on Crime — atrocity data by state; (2) NCSC Annual Reports — for systemic failure documentation; (3) National Law University research on SC/ST Act implementation; (4) NHRC Guidelines on SC/ST protections; (5) Janman People's Foundation training materials and field reports; (6) Bar Council of India rules on professional conduct; (7) Supreme Court judgments — use SCC Online/Indian Kanoon for case law updates. Check for new judgments on the SC/ST Act at least monthly.
