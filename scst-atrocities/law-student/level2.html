📋 1. FIR Obligation and §18A Absolute Bar

The right to register an FIR is the gateway to the entire SC/ST Act protection framework. Denial of FIR registration is the single most common form of institutional betrayal of SC/ST victims, and the law has built multiple layers of protection against it.

⚖ Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of UP (2014 SC — 5-Judge Constitution Bench)

The Constitutional Bench laid down the law definitively: registration of FIR is mandatory for cognizable offences. The police officer has no discretion to refuse, to conduct a preliminary inquiry before registration, or to make a decision on whether the offence is "made out." The FIR must be registered as soon as information of a cognizable offence is received. Post-registration, investigation may reveal the complaint to be false — but registration itself cannot be withheld on the basis of such anticipation. This rule applies with even greater force to SC/ST Act cases, where §18A additionally bars any preliminary inquiry.

The Double Protection: SC/ST Act victims have two layers of mandatory FIR protection — (1) Lalita Kumari's constitutional and common-law mandate that FIR is mandatory for all cognizable offences, and (2) §18A's specific statutory bar on preliminary inquiry before FIR registration in SC/ST Act cases. Any police officer who refuses FIR registration in a SC/ST Act case violates both layers simultaneously and commits an offence under §4 of the SC/ST Act itself.

§4 — FIR Refusal = Criminal Offence: A public servant who wilfully neglects their duties under the SC/ST Act is punishable under §4 — minimum 6 months imprisonment. Refusal to register FIR in a SC/ST Act case is a wilful neglect of duty. File a §4 complaint against the refusing officer. This creates accountability pressure and a paper trail for writ petitions.

Remedies When FIR is Refused — Step-by-Step

  • Written Complaint to SP/SSP under §154(3) CrPC/BNSS
    The first formal step. File a written complaint to the Superintendent of Police or Senior SP of the district, stating that the SHO refused to register FIR and requesting immediate registration. The SP is duty-bound to either direct registration or investigate the refusal. Keep a copy with acknowledgement. This creates the paper trail necessary for the next steps.
  • §156(3) CrPC/BNSS Petition before Judicial Magistrate — The Most Effective Remedy
    File a petition before the Judicial Magistrate of First Class under §156(3) CrPC (§175(3) BNSS) requesting an order directing the police to register FIR and investigate. The Magistrate has the power to direct investigation of any cognizable offence. This is the most legally effective immediate remedy — Magistrate orders cannot be ignored by police without contempt consequences. Include a copy of the SP complaint and any written FIR refusal.
  • Private Complaint under §200 CrPC/BNSS
    File a complaint directly before the Judicial Magistrate under §200 CrPC (§223 BNSS). The Magistrate can examine the complainant on oath and direct the police to investigate or take cognizance directly. This alternative path to the Special Court bypasses the police entirely if necessary. For SC/ST Act cases, the complaint must go to the Special Court Magistrate.
  • Writ of Mandamus under Art.226/Art.32 — For Persistent Refusal
    Where police persistently refuse FIR despite SP complaint and §156(3) order, file a writ of mandamus in the High Court (Art.226) or Supreme Court (Art.32) directing registration of FIR. Ground: statutory duty under §154 CrPC + §18A SC/ST Act + Lalita Kumari mandate. Relief: direction to register FIR within 24/48 hours and report compliance to court. Contempt jurisdiction then protects compliance.
  • NHRC/SHRC/NCSC Complaint — For Systemic Failure and Media Pressure
    File complaints with the National Human Rights Commission (NHRC), State Human Rights Commission (SHRC), and National Commission for Scheduled Castes (NCSC). While these bodies cannot directly compel FIR registration, their notices to state governments create political and administrative pressure. NCSC has specific statutory jurisdiction over SC/ST matters and can recommend prosecution of erring officials.

🔍 2. Investigation Standards — §9 DSP-Level Mandatory Investigation

Section 9 of the SC/ST Act establishes a critical investigative safeguard: only a DSP (Deputy Superintendent of Police) or higher-ranked officer shall investigate SC/ST Act cases. This requirement reflects the legislature's recognition that local-level police — inspectors, sub-inspectors, constables — are deeply embedded in caste hierarchies and cannot be trusted to conduct impartial investigations in atrocity cases.

⚖ Gurinder Singh Aujla v. State of Punjab (2020 SC)

The Supreme Court confirmed that the §9 requirement of DSP-level investigation is mandatory, not directory. A sub-inspector or inspector-level officer completing the investigation of an SC/ST Act case violates §9. Such investigation is procedurally invalid and can be challenged before the Special Court. The Court held that the mandatory nature of §9 reflects the legislature's considered policy choice — senior police accountability is structurally necessary for justice in caste atrocity cases.

Key §9 Investigation Rules

The SC/ST (POA) Rules, 1995, Rule 7 sets out the investigation framework:

Rule 7 — 60-Day Investigation Timeline: The investigating DSP must complete the investigation and submit a report to the Special Court within 60 days of registration of the FIR. The Special Court may extend this period for reasons to be recorded in writing. This time-bound requirement is intended to prevent the chronic delay that characterises SC/ST Act investigations in practice. If the 60-day deadline is missed, file an application before the Special Court noting the delay and seeking directions.

Challenge Strategy — Investigating Officer Below DSP Rank

  • Check IO Rank at the Outset
    At the first opportunity, check the FIR and case diary to determine the rank of the Investigating Officer. Look for the IO's designation — "Sub-Inspector," "Inspector," or "DSP/Deputy SP." If below DSP rank, note this formally in writing.
  • File Application before Special Court — §9 Violation
    File an application before the Special Court challenging the investigation on grounds of §9 violation. The Special Court has the power to direct a fresh investigation by a DSP-rank officer under §173(8) CrPC. Cite Gurinder Singh Aujla — DSP investigation is mandatory, not directory. Seek suspension of existing investigation pending fresh DSP investigation.
  • Seek §173(8) Fresh Investigation / CBI Investigation
    Under §173(8) CrPC (§193(8) BNSS), the Special Court can order further investigation even after charge sheet is filed. In cases of severe bias or community intimidation, file an Art.226 petition seeking transfer of investigation to CBI or State CID. Courts have ordered CBI investigation in cases of police complicity in atrocities.
    • Importance of §9 in Practice: DSP-level investigation matters because: (1) DSPs are less embedded in village-level caste power structures than local SIs/Inspectors; (2) DSPs face greater career accountability for partial investigations; (3) §9 violations give grounds to challenge tainted chargesheet and investigation before the Special Court; (4) in bias cases, the §9 violation combined with one-sided investigation can support writ petitions for CBI investigation.

    📋 3. Evidence Principles in SC/ST Act Cases

    Evidence collection, preservation, and presentation in SC/ST Act cases requires specialised knowledge of both general evidence law and the Act's specific requirements. The key evidence challenge in most cases is establishing the "caste motivation" element — that the act was committed because of the victim's SC/ST identity.

    ✓ DOS — Collect and Secure

    • FIR copy — certified, immediately after registration
    • Medical report — if physical assault; conduct promptly
    • Panchnama of scene of offence — with IO present
    • Witness statements under §164 CrPC (§183 BNSS) before Magistrate — protected, harder to retract
    • Caste certificate of victim — from SDO/DM
    • Caste certificate of accused — from competent authority
    • Proof of caste motivation — community context, prior incidents
    • Electronic evidence — WhatsApp messages, video, audio
    • Expert report on nature of atrocity
    • Revenue records for land-related offences
    • DVMC and SP report on area — for systemic context

    ✗ DON'Ts — Avoid These Mistakes

    • Do not rely solely on hostile witnesses without other corroboration
    • Do not lose documentation chain — certified copies at every stage
    • Do not delay medical examination — delay weakens case
    • Do not let police record statements without §164 CrPC protection
    • Do not underestimate defense "political enmity" argument — prepare rebuttal
    • Do not forget to document caste identity of all accused persons
    • Do not let electronic evidence go uncertified — §65B certificate mandatory
    • Do not skip victim's statement under §164 — it is the spine of the case

    Establishing Caste Motivation — The Critical Element

    In every SC/ST Act case, the prosecution must establish that the act was committed because of the victim's SC/ST identity — i.e., the caste motivation element. This is often the most contested issue at trial. The Supreme Court has provided important guidance:

    Manohar Lal v. State of Madhya Pradesh
    2021 — Supreme Court of India
    The Court held that caste motivation need not be established by direct evidence — it can be inferred from circumstantial evidence. The prosecution is not required to prove that the accused explicitly uttered caste slurs or stated caste as the reason for the act. Where the surrounding circumstances, the identity of the victim, the nature of the act, and the prior relationship between the parties make it clear that caste was the operative motivation, the court can and should draw the inference. This significantly lowers the evidentiary burden on the prosecution and should be cited whenever the defense argues that "caste was not mentioned at the time."

    §164 CrPC Statements — The Gold Standard

    Statements recorded by a Magistrate under §164 CrPC (§183 BNSS) are the most valuable pieces of evidence in an SC/ST Act case because: (1) they are admissible as substantive evidence; (2) they cannot be as easily retracted as police statements; (3) the Magistrate's verification process makes them more credible; (4) they protect against witness tampering after the statement is recorded. As victim's counsel, ensure §164 statement is recorded at the earliest opportunity — ideally within days of FIR registration.

    Cross-Examination Preparation: Defense advocates typically attack SC/ST Act cases on: (i) "Caste not mentioned at time of offence" — counter with Manohar Lal: circumstantial inference of caste motivation; (ii) "Political enmity between parties" — gather evidence of prior caste-based incidents, community status; (iii) "Delay in complaint" — explain delay: fear, community pressure, lack of access to police; (iv) "Witness improvement" — use §164 statements to pin witnesses; (v) "No independent witness" — explain the village social context where SC/ST persons are often isolated. Prepare rehabilitation questions for each anticipated attack.

    ⚖ 4. Bail Regime Analysis — The §18 and §18A Framework

    The bail regime under the SC/ST Act is one of its most important and most contested features. Understanding the precise legal position on each type of bail is essential for both prosecution (opposing bail) and for understanding the rights of accused persons (to ensure no illegal bail grant).

    🚫 Absolutely Barred

    Anticipatory Bail

    §18 bars anticipatory bail. §18A (2018) makes this an absolute bar — no court has discretion. Confirmed in Prathvi Raj Chauhan (2020). Cannot be granted to any accused under the SC/ST Act — including co-accused. Very narrow exception: where on the face of complaint, the complaint is manifestly false and the accused is clearly non-SC/ST — but this exception must not be allowed to become routine.

    ⚠ Discretionary — Oppose Actively

    Regular Bail (Post-Arrest)

    After arrest, the accused can apply to the Special Court for regular bail. The Special Court must consider: gravity of offence, antecedents of accused, social fabric and likelihood of witness tampering, risk to victim. Under §15A(5), victim must be given notice before bail hearing and opportunity to be heard. File bail opposition promptly. Cite caste motivation, risk to witnesses, and victim's vulnerability.

    ✓ Available — Know and Use

    Default Bail (§167 CrPC)

    If charge sheet is not filed within the statutory period (60 days for offences punishable with death/life/10+ years; 90 days for others), the accused is entitled to default bail under §167(2) CrPC (§187 BNSS) on furnishing bail. In Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab (2012 SC), the Court confirmed that default bail is available even in SC/ST Act cases if charge sheet is not filed in time. This is a statutory right — prompt charge sheet filing is therefore essential for victim lawyers.

    Legal Strategy — Opposing Bail in SC/ST Act Cases

    When the accused applies for regular bail: (1) File caveat immediately — ensure victim's counsel is heard before any order is passed (§15A(5) right); (2) Oppose on gravity — §3(2)(v) (rape), §3(2)(iii) (arson) carry minimum life imprisonment possibilities; (3) Cite witness tampering risk — in caste-conflict villages, accused at large poses specific threats; (4) Cite the social fabric — in cases where accused is dominant community member and victim is isolated SC/ST individual, bail will enable intimidation; (5) Cite victim's safety — if bail is granted, victim needs protection measures. File bail opposition in writing with these grounds clearly articulated.

    Mahender Chawla v. Union of India
    2019 — Supreme Court of India
    The Court confirmed the absolute bar on anticipatory bail under §18 of the SC/ST Act and upheld the 2018 Amendment's §18A as a valid exercise of legislative power. The Court carved a very narrow exception: where the complaint is clearly and manifestly false on its face — where it is evident without any further inquiry that the accused is not SC/ST or that the named accused could not possibly have committed the alleged act — regular bail principles may apply. This exception is deliberately narrow and must not be stretched to create a general judicial power to grant anticipatory bail in SC/ST Act cases.
    Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab
    2012 — Supreme Court of India
    Held that the right to default bail under §167(2) CrPC is an indefeasible statutory right that accrues to an accused person if charge sheet is not filed within the prescribed period. This right applies even in SC/ST Act cases — the SC/ST Act does not override §167(2). The implication for victim advocates is critical: ensure charge sheet is filed within the statutory period (60 or 90 days as applicable). If the investigating DSP fails to file charge sheet in time, bring this to the Special Court's attention immediately and seek directions for expedited investigation.

    ⚖ 5. Section 15A — Victim Rights in Comprehensive Detail

    Section 15A, inserted by the 2015 Amendment Act, is the most important provision for victim advocates in the SC/ST Act. It establishes a comprehensive framework of victim rights — procedural, substantive, and remedial. Many of these rights are systematically ignored by police and courts; enforcing them is a primary duty of victim's counsel.

    §15A(1)

    State Protection Duty

    The State Government shall provide protection to victims and witnesses. This includes physical protection (police protection, accommodation in another location), protection from community pressure, and protection from retaliation. File an application before the Special Court or District Magistrate for specific protection measures where victim faces threats.

    §15A(3)

    Right to Legal Aid

    Victim and their dependents are entitled to legal aid. The District Legal Services Authority (DLSA) must provide a lawyer at state expense if the victim cannot afford one. Additionally, under §15A(7), victim can engage their own private counsel who appears alongside the Special Public Prosecutor — at the victim's own expense, but very effective where SPP is under-resourced or not proactive.

    §15A(5)

    Right to Be Heard Before Adverse Orders

    No adverse order — including bail, discharge, acquittal — shall be passed without giving the victim an opportunity to be heard. This is one of the most powerful provisions. File a caveat immediately after FIR registration to ensure notice to victim's counsel before any bail hearing. A bail order passed without hearing the victim under §15A(5) is liable to be quashed by writ of certiorari.

    §15A(6)

    Right to Information

    The victim has the right to information about the progress of the case — FIR registration, charge sheet filing, charges framed, bail applications, trial dates, and judgment. The Investigating Officer and the Special Court are duty-bound to keep the victim informed. File an application before the Special Court if information is being withheld, and supplement with RTI applications to the SP's office.

    §15A(7)

    Right to Private Counsel Alongside SPP

    The victim can be represented by a private advocate in addition to the Special Public Prosecutor. This right is critical where SPPs are overworked, poorly paid, or not fully committed. The private advocate can make arguments, examine/cross-examine witnesses, and ensure victim's interest is properly represented throughout trial. File a vakalatnama in the Special Court on behalf of the victim as private counsel under §15A(7).

    §15A(8)

    Right to Appeal Against Acquittal or Inadequate Sentence

    The victim can prefer an appeal to the High Court against an order of acquittal or against a sentence that is inadequate. This right is independent of the State's (SPP's) right to appeal — even if the SPP does not appeal or expressly decides not to, the victim can file their own appeal. This is transformative — victims are no longer passive observers whose fate depends entirely on SPP decisions.

    §15A(11)

    Right to Participate at Every Stage

    The victim is entitled to participate at every stage of the proceedings — from charge-framing to sentencing. This is not merely a right to be present — it includes the right to make submissions, present arguments, and ensure that victim's perspective is before the court at all stages. Use this right actively during framing of charges, examination of witnesses, final arguments, and especially during sentencing where victim's impact statement should be presented.

    §15A — Compensation

    Right to Compensation

    The SC/ST Rules, 1995 (Schedule I) provide mandatory compensation for each category of offence — payable by the state regardless of conviction. Additionally, §357A CrPC/BNSS enables the Special Court to order compensation from the State Victim Compensation Fund at the time of judgment. Always claim both — interim compensation from state government (DM/Collector) and final compensation from the Special Court at sentencing.

    Practical Checklist for Victim Advocates under §15A: (1) File vakalatnama as private counsel under §15A(7); (2) File caveat before all bail applications — invoke §15A(5); (3) Register victim with DLSA for legal aid under §15A(3); (4) Create a monitoring system for case dates — invoke §15A(6) right to information; (5) File victim impact statement at sentencing stage — invoke §15A(11); (6) Always claim §357A compensation alongside conviction; (7) Advise victim of appeal right under §15A(8) if acquitted or sentence is inadequate. Do all of this from the moment FIR is registered — do not wait until trial.
    Lalita Kumari v. Government of Uttar Pradesh
    2014 — Supreme Court of India (5-Judge Constitution Bench)
    The Constitution Bench unanimously held that registration of FIR is mandatory under §154 CrPC for cognizable offences. The police officer receiving information of a cognizable offence has no discretion to refuse registration or to conduct a preliminary inquiry before registration. The Court expressly held that a preliminary inquiry before registration, other than in exceptional categories specifically identified (matrimonial disputes, etc.), violates the statutory mandate. SC/ST Act cases fall clearly within the mandatory registration category — there is no preliminary inquiry exception. This case, combined with §18A, creates an absolute obligation of immediate FIR registration.
    Gurinder Singh Aujla v. State of Punjab
    2020 — Supreme Court of India
    Confirmed that §9 of the SC/ST Act — requiring investigation by a DSP or higher-ranked officer — is mandatory, not directory. An investigation conducted by an officer below DSP rank is procedurally defective and can be challenged. The Court emphasized that the mandatory nature of §9 reflects a deliberate legislative policy choice to ensure senior police accountability in caste atrocity investigations. Practical implication: always check the rank of the Investigating Officer on the FIR — "Sub-Inspector" or "Inspector" designation on an SC/ST Act FIR is a ground for immediate challenge before the Special Court.
