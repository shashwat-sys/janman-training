Case 1 — The Atrocity Prevention Framework

AIR 1985 SC 1495 | (1985) 3 SCC 580
State of Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale
Supreme Court of India | 5-Judge Constitutional Bench

Facts & Background: The accused, upper-caste villagers, prevented members of a Scheduled Caste community from drawing water from a public well and subjected them to caste-based humiliation. The case reached the Supreme Court on the question of whether such acts constituted "untouchability" under Article 17 and Section 3 of the PCR Act, 1955.

Constitutional Question: Whether "untouchability" under Article 17 includes social practices beyond temple entry and whether a penal law targeting such practices violates Article 21 (personal liberty) of the accused.

Ratio Decidendi: "Untouchability" in Article 17 is not restricted to its traditional sense but must be construed broadly to include all forms of social discrimination based on caste. The PCR Act, 1955 is a special legislation giving effect to constitutional promises and must receive a liberal construction. Denial of water from public sources on caste grounds is an act of untouchability.

The Court held that conviction under the PCR Act does not require proof of physical untouchability — social and economic disabilities imposed by reason of birth in a particular caste suffice.

Doctrinal Contribution: First authoritative ruling expanding the definition of "untouchability" beyond temple entry to encompass economic and social disabilities. Established that the PCR Act must be construed in consonance with the constitutional objective of abolishing untouchability.

Broad construction of "untouchability"
Test Laid Down
Caste-based denial of social rights = untouchability
Continuing Relevance
Applied in manual scavenging and public space exclusion cases

Case 2 — The Anti-Anticipatory Bail Rule

(1994) 4 SCC 260 | 1994 SCC (Cri) 1237
State of M.P. v. Ram Krishna Balothia
Supreme Court of India | Constitution Bench

Facts & Background: The respondent, accused of offences under the SC/ST (POA) Act, 1989, applied for anticipatory bail. Section 18 of the POA Act expressly bars Section 438 CrPC (anticipatory bail) in atrocity cases. The High Court granted anticipatory bail holding Section 18 was unconstitutional as it violates personal liberty under Article 21.

Constitutional Question: Whether Section 18 of the POA Act, which bars anticipatory bail, is constitutionally valid or violates Articles 14, 19, and 21 of the Constitution.

Ratio Decidendi: Section 18 of the POA Act is constitutionally valid. It does not violate Articles 14, 19, or 21 of the Constitution. The Constitution makers specifically created Article 17 to abolish untouchability — Parliament has the competence and indeed the constitutional duty to enact stringent special laws to give effect to this mandate. The bar on anticipatory bail is a reasonable restriction serving the compelling state interest of protecting SC/ST persons from atrocities.

There is a rational basis for treating atrocity offences differently from ordinary criminal cases — victims of atrocities are in a position of vulnerability requiring special protection. Differential treatment is not discrimination but compensation for historical injustice.

Doctrinal Contribution: Established the constitutional validity of special protective provisions under the POA Act. The "compelling state interest" doctrine in the SC/ST context. This judgment was the primary precedent relied upon when Section 18A (introduced by 2018 Amendment) was challenged in Prathvi Raj Chauhan.

Later Development: When the Supreme Court in Subhash Kashinath Mahajan (2018) effectively weakened Section 18 by reading in a preliminary inquiry requirement, Ram Krishna Balothia was distinguished. In Prathvi Raj Chauhan (2020), a 3-judge bench restored the full effect of Section 18, re-affirming Ram Krishna Balothia as good law.

Section Upheld
Section 18 POA Act — no anticipatory bail
Basis
Article 17 constitutional mandate; compelling state interest
Current Status
Good law; confirmed by Prathvi Raj Chauhan (2020)

Case 3 — Special Courts and Speedy Trial

(2004) 5 SCC 518 | AIR 2004 SC 2605
Gangula Ashok v. State of Andhra Pradesh
Supreme Court of India | 3-Judge Bench

Facts & Background: An atrocity case was tried by a Sessions Court that had been designated as an "Exclusive Special Court" under Section 14 of the POA Act. The question was whether a non-exclusive Special Court (a Sessions Court with general jurisdiction, designated to additionally handle POA cases) had jurisdiction to try atrocity offences, or whether only an exclusive Special Court could do so.

Ratio Decidendi: The POA Act mandates that atrocity offences be tried exclusively by Special Courts established under Section 14. The designation of an existing Sessions Court as a Special Court for POA purposes is valid, but such court must try POA cases with priority and cannot treat them as ordinary sessions cases to be dealt with in routine course. Speedy trial is a constitutional mandate in atrocity cases given the vulnerability of SC/ST victims.

Critically, the Court held that ordinary Sessions Courts (without any designation under Section 14) have no jurisdiction to try POA Act offences — if they do so, the trial is void ab initio.

Doctrinal Contribution: Settled the jurisdictional question — POA trials must occur in designated Special Courts, not ordinary courts. This has significant procedural implications: a conviction by a non-designated court can be challenged on jurisdictional grounds even at the appeal/revision stage.

Level 2 Assessment

20 questions · Pass mark: 14/20 (70%) · Answers locked after submission

Q1In Karnataka v. Appa Balu Ingale (1985), what was the Supreme Court's construction of "untouchability" under Article 17?
The Court in Appa Balu Ingale held that "untouchability" under Article 17 must be broadly construed to include all forms of caste-based social and economic disability, not just temple entry. Denial of water from public wells on caste grounds was held to be an act of untouchability.
Q2In State of M.P. v. Ram Krishna Balothia (1994), Section 18 of the POA Act was challenged as unconstitutional. The Supreme Court held:
Ram Krishna Balothia (1994) is the foundation case upholding Section 18's bar on anticipatory bail as constitutional, serving the compelling state interest under Article 17 to abolish untouchability. Option (d) was the incorrect position taken by Subhash Kashinath (2018), now reversed by Section 18A.
Q3Under Gangula Ashok v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2004), if an atrocity case is tried by a Sessions Court that has not been designated as a Special Court under Section 14 of the POA Act, the trial is:
Gangula Ashok held that only courts designated as Special Courts under Section 14 of the POA Act have jurisdiction to try atrocity offences. A non-designated Sessions Court conducting such a trial acts without jurisdiction — the trial is void ab initio.
Q4The "caste motivation" doctrine in Swaran Singh v. State (2008) holds that for an offence to attract the POA Act:
bel>
Swaran Singh clarified that while caste motivation is required, it need not be proved by direct evidence alone. Courts can infer caste motivation from the totality of circumstances — the nature of the insult, words used (caste-specific slurs), social context, and prior relations between parties.
Q12Section 15A of the POA Act (2016 Amendment) codified victim rights recognized in cases like Sube Singh. Which of the following is NOT a right under Section 15A?
Section 15A provides rights to be informed of the case, to be heard at bail hearings, and to receive State-provided legal assistance. Option (d) — "automatic acquittal review" — is not a right under Section 15A or any provision of the POA Act. Acquittal appeals are filed by the State under Sections 374-377 CrPC/BNSS.
Q13What was the constitutional basis for Parliament's competence to enact the bar on anticipatory bail under Section 18, as held in Ram Krishna Balothia?
Ram Krishna Balothia relied on Article 17 (abolition of untouchability) read with Article 35 (Parliament's power to make laws giving effect to fundamental rights). Parliament has not merely the power but the constitutional duty to enact stringent measures to fulfil the Article 17 mandate, including bars on anticipatory bail.
Q14In Gangula Ashok (2004), what is the position regarding a Sessions Court that is designated as a Special Court under Section 14 but has general jurisdiction over ordinary criminal cases?
Gangula Ashok held that a Sessions Court with general criminal jurisdiction CAN be designated as a Special Court under Section 14 — it need not be exclusive. However, the designation must be expressly made, and POA cases must be given priority. Post-2016, Section 14A additionally mandates Exclusive Special Courts where average pending cases exceed 100 annually.
Q15Which of the three Subhash Kashinath (2018) directions was most directly overridden by the 2018 Amendment to the POA Act?
Section 18A, inserted by the 2018 Amendment, specifically overrides all three Subhash Kashinath directions: (i) No preliminary inquiry before FIR registration; (ii) No prior approval of any authority required before arrest; (iii) Section 18's absolute bar on anticipatory bail is fully restored.
Q16The 2016 offences added to the POA Act (Sections 3(1)(r), (s), (t), (u), (v), (w), etc.) are significant in relation to Swaran Singh's caste motivation requirement because:
The 2016 new offences (social/economic boycott, preventing SC/ST from using common resources, electoral intimidation, etc.) are inherently caste-linked — the very nature of these acts establishes caste motivation. This addresses the evidentiary difficulty created by Swaran Singh's motivation requirement, while not eliminating it entirely.
Q17In which case did the Supreme Court hold that a State law providing 50% sub-reservation for Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs within the SC category was constitutionally valid?
Davinder Singh (2024) — a 7-Judge Constitutional bench — held that the Punjab law sub-classifying the SC reservation for Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs was valid, and overruled E.V. Chinnaiah's "homogeneity" doctrine to the extent it prevented such sub-classification.
Q18The intersection of manual scavenging and the POA Act, clarified in Safai Karamchari Andolan (2014), means that compelling an SC person to continue scavenging by reason of caste can additionally attract which POA Act provisions (as per pre-2016 numbering)?
The judgment in Safai Karamchari Andolan identified that forcing an SC/ST person to perform scavenging by reason of caste can attract Section 3(1)(f) (compelling to do forced/begar work) and Section 3(1)(xi) (insult or humiliation in public view), in addition to the Manual Scavengers Act provisions.
Q19Before the Davinder Singh (2024) judgment, what was the position established in E.V. Chinnaiah (2004) regarding the Presidential SC list?
E.V. Chinnaiah (2004) held that the Presidential SC list under Article 341 is a "homogenous class" — since only Parliament can modify it, States cannot sub-classify it for preferential treatment within the SC quota. This position was overruled by the 7-Judge bench in Davinder Singh (2024).
Q20Which judgment established that the principle of "compelling state interest" justifies special protective provisions under the POA Act that may appear to treat accused persons differently from ordinary criminal cases?
Ram Krishna Balothia (1994) is the foundational case that introduced the "compelling state interest" doctrine in the SC/ST context. The Court held that Article 17's mandate creates a compelling state interest sufficient to justify special protective provisions that treat atrocity offences differently from ordinary crimes.
