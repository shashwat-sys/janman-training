Level 1: Constitutional Foundations & Statutory Analysis

Art. 17, Arts. 341-342, complete analysis of PCR Act 1955 and POA Act 1989, constitutional validity, definitional jurisprudence and offence taxonomy

Constitutional Architecture of SC/ST Protection

Article 17 — Abolition of Untouchability

"Untouchability" is abolished and its practice in any form is forbidden. The enforcement of any disability arising out of Untouchability shall be an offence punishable in accordance with law.

Academic note: Art. 17 is unique — it creates an enforceable fundamental right against private persons, not just the state. In Akhil Bharatiya Soshit Karmachari Sangh v. Union of India (1981), the SC held Art. 17 operates horizontally, binding all citizens.

Definitional Ambiguity — "Untouchability"

The Constitution does not define "untouchability." The Supreme Court in Devarajiah v. B. Padmanna (1958) held the term should be understood in its historical sociological context — the social disability imposed on certain castes from birth based on Hindu religious practice. The PCR Act 1955 operationalises this constitutional mandate.

PCR Act 1955 — Analytical Framework

The Protection of Civil Rights Act 1955 (amended 1976) implements Art. 17. It penalises the enforcement of disabilities arising from untouchability — the social/civil dimension, distinct from the physical violence dimension covered by POA Act.

POA Act 1989 — Constitutional Validity & Objectives

Preamble & Objects

The Statement of Objects and Reasons notes that existing laws (IPC, CrPC, PCR Act) have proved inadequate to check atrocities committed on members of Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes. The Act aims to prevent such atrocities, provide special courts for speedy trial, and provide relief and rehabilitation.

Constitutional Validity

State of Maharashtra v. Bhaurao Punjabrao Gawande — (2008) 3 SCC 613
Scope and Strict Interpretation of Scheduled Offences
SC held that the POA Act must be given liberal construction in keeping with its object — prevention of atrocities. The essential ingredient of caste identity (victim SC/ST, accused non-SC/ST) is a jurisdictional prerequisite. Courts cannot water down this requirement but also cannot expand the statute beyond its terms.
Swaran Singh v. State — (2008) 8 SCC 435
Essential Ingredients — Caste Element is Jurisdictional
Supreme Court clarified: (1) The accused must be a non-SC/ST; (2) The offence must be committed against an SC/ST; (3) The offence must be committed "on the ground that the victim is an SC/ST" — the caste motivation is essential. A simple assault between two persons of different castes does not attract POA Act unless the caste of victim was the motivation for the offence.

2016 & 2018 Amendments — Academic Analysis

Significance of the 2016 Amendment Act

The 2016 Amendment was a direct response to judicial dilution of the Act's provisions. Key academic points:

Sec 18A — Response to Judicial Overreach: The amendment overturned the practice of requiring preliminary inquiries before FIR registration — a practice courts had developed despite the Act's silence on it. Parliament expressly prohibited such inquiries.

Sec 15A — Victim Rights Chapter: Borrowed from comparative victimology and international standards (UN Declaration on Victims 1985), this section created the most comprehensive victim rights provision in Indian criminal law.

19 New Offences: Expanded coverage to digital/modern forms of caste discrimination (social media humiliation) and addressed lacunae identified by the National Crime Records Bureau data.

The Constitutional Crisis — Subhash Kashinath and Recovery

In Subhash Kashinath Mahajan v. State of Maharashtra (2018) 6 SCC 454, a 2-judge bench introduced a preliminary inquiry requirement and SP approval for arrest — effectively creating protections for accused not in the statute. This triggered massive protests. Parliament responded with the 2018 Amendment (Statement of Objects: "to restore the original intent") and the Supreme Court in Prathvi Raj Chauhan v. UOI (2020) 4 SCC 727 (5-judge bench) recalled Subhash Kashinath and upheld the original provisions.

📝 Level 1 Assessment — 20 Questions (Pass: 70%)

Q1. Article 17 of the Constitution operates as a fundamental right enforceable against:
Art. 17 is unique — it has horizontal application against private persons. The SC in Akhil Bharatiya Soshit Karmachari Sangh v. UOI (1981) confirmed this. PCR Act enforces Art. 17 against private untouchability practitioners.
Q2. The constitutional definition of "untouchability" in Art. 17 is:
Art. 17 does not define "untouchability." Devarajiah v. B. Padmanna (1958) held it must be understood in its historical context — the caste-based social disability imposed from birth in Hindu religious practice.
Q3. In E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of AP (2004), the Supreme Court held that state sub-classification of Scheduled Castes for reservation is:
E.V. Chinnaiah (2004) held sub-classification unconstitutional. This was partially overruled by a 7-judge bench in State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024) which held states CAN sub-classify — a live constitutional controversy.
Q4. The essential "caste motivation" element in POA Act offences was articulated in:
Swaran Singh v. State (2008) 8 SCC 435 held that the offence must be committed because the victim is an SC/ST — the caste of the victim must be the motivation, not merely incidental to the offence.
Q5. Which section of the PCR Act creates a presumption of untouchability motive when disability is enforced against an SC person?
PCR Act Sec 14 — presumption of untouchability motive. When it is established that the victim belongs to an SC community and that a civil disability was enforced, the court presumes untouchability motivation — shifting burden to accused.
Q6. "Devadasi dedication" of SC/ST women — forcing them into religious prostitution — is an atrocity under:
Sec 3(1)(p) specifically covers the practice of dedicating SC/ST women as devadasis, Jogins, or similar customary practices — recognizing this as both a religious and caste-based form of sexual exploitation.
Q7. The "objects and reasons" of the POA Act 1989 specifically states that which existing laws proved inadequate?
The Statement of Objects expressly identifies the IPC, CrPC, and PCR Act 1955 as having proven inadequate to check atrocities — this legislative history is important for liberal interpretation of the POA Act.
Q8. The addition of Sec 15A to the POA Act (2016) was influenced by which international standard?
Sec 15A's victim rights provisions — right to information, to be heard, protection of identity, safety, relocation — are modelled on the UN Declaration on Basic Principles of Justice for Victims of Crime and Abuse of Power (1985).
Q9. Which category of offences under the POA Act addresses the denial of common property resources?
Social exclusion offences — particularly Sec 3(1)(e) (denial of waterways/roads), Sec 3(1)(h) (polluting water sources), Sec 3(1)(u) (denial of common burial grounds, pasture) — address common property resource denial.
Q10. In Subhash Kashinath Mahajan (2018), the Supreme Court's controversial addition was:
Subhash Kashinath Mahajan (2018) 6 SCC 454 introduced preliminary inquiry and SP approval — creating safeguards not in the statute. Prathvi Raj Chauhan (2020) 5-judge bench recalled these additions and upheld Sec 18A.
Q11. Art. 341(2) provides that inclusion/exclusion of communities in the SC list is the function of:
Art. 341(2) provides that Parliament may by law include or exclude communities — this is a legislative function. Courts cannot judicially add or remove communities from the Presidential SC list (SC: Palghat Jilla Thandan Samudhaya Maha Sabha v. UOI, 1993).
Q12. The 7-judge bench ruling in State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh (2024) is significant because it:
Davinder Singh (2024) 7-judge bench held states have the power to sub-classify within Scheduled Castes for the purpose of reservations — partially overruling E.V. Chinnaiah (2004) — a landmark development in SC/ST constitutional law.
Q13. The PCR Act 1955 tries offences through which court?
PCR Act offences are tried by Executive Magistrates (First Class) — unlike the POA Act which requires Special Courts (Sessions level). This is an important procedural distinction for legal practitioners.
Q14. "Wilful neglect" under Sec 4 POA Act — what test do courts apply?
Courts apply an objective standard — was the officer's omission deliberate or so grossly negligent as to amount to wilfulness? This covers malicious inaction and reckless disregard of legal duty, not mere oversight.
Q15. The academic distinction between PCR Act and POA Act is best described as:
PCR Act: enforcement of civil/social disabilities arising from untouchability (access to places, services). POA Act: physical, sexual, economic and psychological atrocities. Both are criminal statutes but with different subject matter.
Q16. Sec 16 of the POA Act (collective fine) raises constitutional concerns under which article?
Collective fine raises concerns under Art. 14 (imposing liability on entire community) and Art. 20(1) (individual criminal culpability principle). Courts have interpreted Sec 16 narrowly — requiring knowledge/connivance of the community, not mere presence.
Q17. The POA Act's Sec 15A victim rights provision was introduced specifically to address:
Sec 15A addresses secondary victimisation — the additional trauma inflicted on victims during criminal process through intimidation, identity exposure, lack of information, and exclusion from proceedings. It creates substantive procedural rights.
Q18. For the POA Act to apply, the accused person need NOT be which of the following?
The POA Act does not require the accused to be from the same village — they simply must be non-SC/ST, act against an SC/ST person, with caste as the motivation. Residential proximity is not an element of the offence.
Q19. Sec 18A(1) of the POA Act expressly prohibits:
Sec 18A(1) provides: "Notwithstanding anything contained in the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973, a preliminary enquiry shall not be required for registration of a First Information Report against any person under the provisions of this Act."
Q20. Which of the following correctly describes the relationship between the IPC and the POA Act?
The POA Act is a special statute that supplements the IPC — charges under both can and should be filed simultaneously. The special law's enhanced punishment and special court provisions apply alongside IPC provisions (Generalia specialibus non derogant).
