⚖️ Strategic Litigation in GBV Cases

1. Criminal Litigation — Strengthening the Prosecution

In GBV cases, the lawyer or paralegal assisting a survivor must understand how to:

  • Monitor the investigation: Track whether the FIR is registered under the correct sections (e.g., BNS §63/64 for rape, not just §74 for harassment)
  • Oppose bail strategically: File opposition with specific grounds — gravity of offence, threat to witness, likelihood of absconding, prior criminal record
  • Victim's right under BNSS §399: The victim can be heard at bail hearings in offences punishable with 7+ years imprisonment
  • Participate in trial: Engage a private counsel under §301 CrPC (now §341 BNSS) to assist the Public Prosecutor with survivor's consent
Key provision: Under BNSS §399(3), a victim has the right to engage an advocate of their choice to oppose bail. The court MUST hear the victim's lawyer before granting bail in cases with 7+ year punishment.

2. PIL and Writ Petitions

PILs are powerful tools to challenge systemic failures. File under:

  • Article 32 — Supreme Court (fundamental rights violation)
  • Article 226 — High Court (broader jurisdiction, including mandamus, certiorari)

Successful GBV PILs have achieved:

  • Guidelines for rape survivor support (Nipun Saxena v UoI, 2018)
  • One-stop centre establishment mandates
  • Mandatory POSH committees in all districts
  • Action on sewer and sanitation worker deaths
Locus standi: Any person can file a PIL if the issue affects a class of disadvantaged persons. The petitioner need not be the direct victim. Courts have accepted NGOs, lawyers, and journalists as petitioners.

3. Anticipatory Bail Opposition

Under §482 BNSS (formerly §438 CrPC), accused can seek anticipatory bail. To oppose:

  • File counter-affidavit citing: severity of offence, ongoing threat to survivor, custodial interrogation required for evidence
  • Cite: Sushila Aggarwal v State (NCT of Delhi) (2020) — SC held A/B can be granted with conditions, but courts must consider severity of accusation
  • Invoke §482(3) BNSS: court SHALL hear the Public Prosecutor and may add conditions

5. The Intervention of Amicus and Third-Party Intervenors

In high-impact cases, organizations like Janman can apply for Intervenor status. This allows filing of additional written submissions and supporting the survivor's position. The court may allow it if the intervenor has relevant expertise or the matter involves a class of persons.

🔬 Evidence Collection & Documentation

1. Medico-Legal Evidence

Medical examination is governed by the Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Act §27 (for minors) and general criminal procedure. Key rules:

  • Medical examination must be conducted by a registered medical practitioner within 24 hours of complaint
  • For rape survivors: examination by female doctor in presence of another woman unless survivor requests otherwise
  • Two-finger test (virginity test) is ILLEGAL: Lillu @ Rajesh v State of Haryana (2013) — SC declared it violates dignity. Any doctor conducting it can face disciplinary action.
  • MLC (Medico-Legal Certificate) should record all injuries; absence of physical injury does NOT mean no rape occurred
  • Delay in examination: Courts have consistently held delay alone does not invalidate the case (State of UP v Munshi (2009))
Critical: Ensure the MLC is obtained and a copy is given to the survivor. Police have a duty to take the survivor to a government hospital immediately. Private hospital charges cannot be imposed on the survivor for medico-legal examination (BNSS §357C).

2. Digital Evidence

Digital evidence is increasingly central in stalking, harassment, and POCSO cases:

  • Screenshots and WhatsApp messages: Preserve originals; forward to a secure email. Screenshots alone may not be admissible without certification under §65B of the Indian Evidence Act (now §63 BSA 2023).
  • §63 BSA 2023: Electronic records admissible only with a certificate from the person responsible for the device/network confirming authenticity
  • Call Detail Records (CDR): Can be obtained by police from telecom companies; can establish proximity at crime scene
  • CCTV footage: Immediately apply in writing to preserve; footage is typically overwritten in 30 days
  • Social media evidence: Notarized screenshots with metadata (date/time/URL) are stronger; reports to NCRB Cyber Crime Portal create an official record

3. Witness Protection

Under the Witness Protection Scheme 2018 (SC-approved in Mahender Chawla v UoI (2018)):

  • Witness can apply to the Witness Protection Cell for identity protection, relocation, change of phone number
  • Threat analysis is done and a protection order issued within 5 days
  • Applicable to: witnesses in cases with 7+ year punishment or death penalty
  • Survivor in rape/POCSO cases is automatically covered as a protected witness

4. Chain of Custody

Any physical evidence (clothing, samples, weapons) must maintain strict chain of custody:

  • Every transfer of evidence must be documented with signature and timestamp
  • Sealed evidence must not be opened except by authorized persons at the FSL
  • Broken chain of custody is a classic defence argument — ensure investigating officer maintains records

5. Dying Declaration

Under §26 BSA 2023 (formerly §32(1) IEA), a statement made by a person believing death to be imminent is admissible. Key principles:

  • Can be oral, written, or by gestures
  • Best if recorded by a Magistrate; if not possible, doctor or police officer can record
  • Multiple dying declarations: if consistent, all are admissible (Laxman v State of Maharashtra (2002))
  • No corroboration required if the declaration is trustworthy (Paniben v State of Gujarat (1992))
Practice tip: If you learn a dowry death victim has given a dying declaration to a doctor or neighbour, immediately get an affidavit from that witness before they are pressured to retract.

6. Character Evidence and Prior Sexual History

Section 53A BSA 2023 (formerly §155(4) IEA — now repealed) prohibits use of the survivor's sexual history or "character" to impeach credibility in rape trials. The defence CANNOT suggest the survivor is of "immoral character." Any lawyer who does so should be objected to immediately.

✍️ Drafting Skills for GBV Cases

1. Representation for FIR Registration

When police refuse to register FIR, draft a representation to the Superintendent of Police (SP). Essential elements:

  • Header: To: SP [District], CC: DIG, NHRC, State Human Rights Commission
  • Factual narration: Date/time/place of incident, names of accused, witnesses, nature of offence in plain language
  • Legal provisions violated: Cite specific BNS sections and Lalita Kumari v Govt of UP (2013) — mandatory FIR registration for cognizable offences
  • Relief sought: Registration of FIR, arrest of accused, protection of survivor, medical examination
  • Enclosures: Medical certificate, photographs, previous complaints if any
Lalita Kumari Principle: If information received by police discloses commission of a cognizable offence, registration of FIR is MANDATORY. Police have no discretion to conduct preliminary enquiry in cases of rape, murder, child sexual assault.

2. Complaint Before ICC (POSH)

A written complaint to the Internal Complaints Committee must include:

  • Name, designation, and contact of complainant and respondent
  • Detailed description of each incident (date, time, place, exact words/acts)
  • Names of witnesses present, if any
  • Documents attached: screenshots, emails, call logs
  • Prayer: inquiry, interim relief (transfer of respondent, work-from-home), appropriate action

File within 3 months of the last incident; ICC can extend by another 3 months for good cause.

3. Application for Protection Order (PWDVA)

File before the Judicial Magistrate 1st Class (JMFC) as DV Application under §12 PWDVA. The DV application should include:

  • Incident Report (DIR) filed by Protection Officer or self
  • Description of domestic relationships (shared household)
  • All forms of violence: physical, sexual, verbal, emotional, economic
  • Relief sought under §§18, 19, 20, 21, 22, 23 (as applicable)
  • Ex-parte application: request for immediate interim protection order under §23

4. Bail Opposition Application

Structure of bail opposition in rape/POCSO/dowry death cases:

  1. Nature and gravity of offence (cite BNS section and maximum punishment)
  2. Character of the accused (prior criminal record, if any)
  3. Likelihood of fleeing justice (no fixed address, travel history, passport)
  4. Threat to witness and survivor (direct evidence of intimidation, calls, threats)
  5. Need for continued custody for investigation (test identification parade, recovery of weapon)
  6. Case law: cite relevant SC decisions on bail parameters in serious sexual offences
Never agree to bail without consulting the survivor. Under BNSS §399, the survivor has a right to be heard. Advocate their position clearly.

5. Compensation Application Before DLSA

Under NALSA Victim Compensation Scheme / State Victim Compensation Scheme (SVCS), apply to DLSA for compensation:

  • Can be filed by victim, her counsel, or any NGO on her behalf
  • Application must include: certified copy of FIR, medical report, loss of income/livelihood assessment
  • Interim compensation can be sought within 8 weeks of application
  • State-wise tables: Bihar SVCS provides up to ₹3 lakh for gang rape, up to ₹5 lakh for acid attack
  • Courts can also direct compensation under §357 BNSS even if accused is acquitted

6. Written Arguments (Purvis in Trial)

After evidence recording, the court calls for written arguments. Effective written arguments:

  • Begin with facts as proved by prosecution evidence
  • Connect each ingredient of offence to specific evidence on record
  • Address defence arguments proactively
  • Cite relevant case law for each proposition
  • End with: "It is therefore humbly submitted that the accused be convicted under §___ BNS and sentenced appropriately, and compensation be awarded under §357 BNSS."

📚 Landmark Case Studies

1. Vishaka & Ors v State of Rajasthan
AIR 1997 SC 3011 | Supreme Court of India | J. Verma, Sujata Manohar, B.N. Kirpal JJ.

Bhanwari Devi, a Saathin (village-level worker) in Rajasthan, was gang-raped in 1992 after she tried to prevent a child marriage. The police initially refused to file an FIR. A PIL was filed by women's rights organizations.

Principle: The Supreme Court laid down the Vishaka Guidelines — the first binding sexual harassment guidelines in workplaces — treating sexual harassment as a violation of fundamental rights under Arts. 14, 19, 21. These became the basis of the POSH Act 2013.
2. Nipun Saxena v Union of India
(2018) 2 SCC 454 | Supreme Court | Dipak Misra CJ, A.M. Khanwilkar, D.Y. Chandrachud JJ.

A public interest petition focused on the identity and privacy protection of rape and POCSO survivors in media and court proceedings.

Principle: Courts must not name survivors in judgments, orders, or media reports. FIRs in rape cases must not be widely circulated. High Courts must redact survivor identities. Courts must appoint support persons for survivor-witnesses.
3. Satish Chander Ahuja v Sneha Ahuja
(2020) 17 SCC 185 | Supreme Court | Ashok Bhushan, R. Subhash Reddy, M.R. Shah JJ.

The husband claimed the wife had no right to remain in his parents' property (the shared household was ancestral/HUF property, not matrimonial home).

Principle: "Shared household" under PWDVA §2(s) means the house where the aggrieved woman lives or has lived in a domestic relationship — it need not be owned by the husband. She has a right of residence even in in-laws' property. Overruled the earlier SR Batra judgment.
4. Independent Thought v Union of India
(2017) 10 SCC 800 | Supreme Court | Madan B. Lokur, Deepak Gupta JJ.

A PIL challenging the marital rape exception under the old IPC §375 Exception 2, which protected men who had sex with their wives aged 15-18.

Principle: Sexual intercourse with a wife aged 15-18 years is rape. The exception was struck down as violating Arts. 14, 15, 21. (Note: full marital rape with adult wives remains unaddressed in law.)
5. Shafin Jahan v Asokan K.M. (Hadiya Case)
(2018) 16 SCC 368 | Supreme Court | Dipak Misra CJ, A.M. Khanwilkar, D.Y. Chandrachud JJ.

A woman's right to choose her religion and spouse was challenged by her parents who alleged "love jihad."

Principle: An adult woman's right to choose her partner is an inalienable fundamental right under Art. 21. Neither courts nor parents can annul a marriage on grounds of inter-faith conversion. The High Court's annulment order was set aside.
6. Society for Enlightenment and Voluntary Action v Union of India
WP(C) 382/2015 | Supreme Court | (Judgment 2024)

A PIL challenging the validity of child marriages performed under personal law codes, particularly where religious customs permit early marriage.

Principle: PCMA 2006 applies to ALL communities regardless of personal law. Child marriages are voidable (and void for minors under 18/21). Religious personal laws cannot override the secular PCMA. The SC directed strict enforcement and awareness campaigns.

🗓️ Recent Judgments (2023–2025)

Supreme Court

Prajjwal Revanna Case — Survivor Dignity in Media
Supreme Court — Suo Motu, 2024
Ruling: The SC issued guidelines banning media from airing graphic videos of sexual assault. Restated that publication of material identifying or degrading survivors violates §23 POCSO and §228A IPC (now BNS §72). YouTube and social media platforms directed to take down such content within 24 hours.
X v State of Kerala — POCSO and Age Verification
(2023) Supreme Court
Ruling: Where a person genuinely believed the victim was above 18, it is not a complete defence under POCSO, but may mitigate sentence. Courts must adopt a child-sensitive approach and prioritize the best interest of the child in sentencing.
Ramji Lal Bairwa v State of Rajasthan — Bail in Rape Cases
(2024) Supreme Court
Ruling: Bail in rape cases must not be granted mechanically. Courts MUST consider: (1) survivor's safety, (2) risk of witness tampering, (3) public interest in prosecution. HC orders granting bail without analysis set aside.
Anuj Garg v Hotel Association — POSH and Employer Liability
Reiterated in 2024 POSH enforcement proceedings
Ruling: Employers cannot escape POSH liability by outsourcing employees to contractors. The "employer" under POSH includes the entity that controls the work environment. Organizations without ICC face ₹50,000 fine; repeat violations lead to licence cancellation.

Patna High Court — Bihar Developments

CWJC 4211/2023 — Compensation for Acid Attack Survivor
Patna High Court, 2024
Ruling: Patna HC directed the State to pay ₹3 lakh interim compensation to an acid attack survivor within 4 weeks, and ensure free plastic surgery at IGIMS Patna. Cited the Supreme Court's guidelines in Laxmi v UoI (2014).
Criminal Misc. — Zero FIR and BNSS Implementation
Patna High Court, 2024
Ruling: Directed all police stations in Bihar to accept Zero FIRs under BNSS §173 without delay. DGP issued circular following HC direction. Failure to register Zero FIR is now treated as misconduct under State Police Act.
