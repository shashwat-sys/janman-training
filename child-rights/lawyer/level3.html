Bail, Compensation, PIL & Constitutional Remedies

SECTION 1: Bail in Child Rights Cases

POCSO Bail Framework

POCSO does not contain any specific bail restriction provision. Therefore, general CrPC provisions apply: Section 437 (bail by police/magistrate) and Section 439 (bail by court/HC).

However, courts exercise heightened caution in POCSO cases. Bail is available but courts examine:

— Nature and gravity of offence (penetrative assault vs. harassment)
— Age of victim (younger children = stronger ground to deny bail)
— Accused's criminal antecedents
— Flight risk assessment
— Witness tampering risk (living in same locality)
— Victim's safety and fear after accused release
— Strength of prosecution case at bail stage
— No specific statutory bar to bail in POCSO; but judicial reluctance common

Victim's Right to Oppose Bail

Per Asha Ranjan v. State of Bihar, victim has constitutional locus to oppose bail. Procedure:

Filing Victim Opposition to Bail:

— Engage victim counsel at bail stage

— File sworn objection statement (not petition)

— Highlight: child's fear, accused's threat history, same locality residence

— Assert Art 21 interest in personal safety

— Reference Asha Ranjan for victim's locus

— Court will hear victim counsel before granting bail

Anticipatory Bail in POCSO Cases

Anticipatory bail available under Section 438 CrPC; but courts very reluctant. To obtain:

— Prove absence of prima facie case
— Show disproportionality (serious investigation, weak evidence)
— Conditions often stringent: daily reporting, no contact with victim/witnesses, passport surrender

Bail Conditions When Granted

Standard conditions in POCSO cases:

— Stay away from victim (minimum 1 km radius, often entire locality)
— Not to approach or contact witnesses
— Surrender passport
— Regular police reporting (weekly/twice-weekly)
— No communication with family of victim without court permission

High Court Jurisdiction for Bail (Section 439/BNSS Sec 483)

If trial court denies bail, HC has inherent power under Section 439 to grant bail. Challenge strategy:

— File Writ Petition in HC
— Show trial court's error in appreciation of Section 437 conditions
— Emphasize accused's right to liberty vs. prosecution convenience
— HC often grants bail in strong cases where trial court too cautious

SECTION 2: Victim Compensation Strategy

Combined Compensation Approach

Child victims have multiple routes to compensation:

1. Section 357A CrPC (BNSS Section 395) — Criminal court directs compensation during trial
2. POCSO Rules 2020 Rule 7 — Compensation guidelines specific to POCSO offences
3. NALSA Scheme — National Legal Services Authority victim compensation scheme (state-wise)
4. State Victim Compensation Schemes — Many states have enhanced amounts for child victims

Filing for Compensation

Before Trial Court (Preferred):

— File application under Section 357A during evidence/final arguments stage

— Attach medical report, school certificates (education interrupted), income loss if applicable

— Specify quantum with justification

— Court can order provisional/interim compensation even during trial

Before District Legal Services Authority (DLSA):

— Post-conviction, apply to DLSA if trial court compensation inadequate

— DLSA can award enhanced compensation from state/NALSA fund

— Even on acquittal, DLSA can award compensation (Section 357A permits)

Interim Compensation (POCSO Rules 2020 Rule 7)

Child victim can apply for immediate interim compensation at any stage of trial (even before trial begins):

— Application to Special Court citing POCSO Rules 2020 Rule 7
— No conviction required; injury/trauma sufficient
— Court directs state to immediately pay relief amount
— Typically ₹5,000-₹50,000 depending on injury severity

Quantum of Compensation

Child victims receive enhanced compensation under most state schemes:

— Bihar: ₹5 lakhs for POCSO Sec 7 (sexual assault); ₹10 lakhs for Sec 3/5 (penetrative/aggravated)
— UP: ₹3-8 lakhs depending on offence and injury
— Delhi: ₹7-10 lakhs for aggravated offences
— Factors: nature of offence, injury degree, lost education, psychological trauma
— Interim compensation: ₹20,000-₹2 lakhs for immediate relief

Compensation Even on Acquittal

Section 357A permits compensation award even if accused acquitted, if court believes victim suffered injury. Strategy:

— Despite acquittal, file Section 357A application
— Emphasize investigative/trial process trauma, medical expenses, school interruption
— Reference: Maru Ram v. UOI — compensation available on acquittal if victim proved suffering

SECTION 3: PIL Strategy for Child Rights

When to File PIL vs. Individual Writ

File PIL when:

— Mass violations (e.g., systemic CCI abuse, multiple missing children)
— Systemic failure (e.g., POCSO trial delay across state)
— Government inaction (e.g., no SJPU formed despite mandate)
— Structural remedies needed (e.g., CCI inspection protocol overhaul)

File Individual Writ when:

— Single child needs immediate relief (habeas corpus for wrongful detention)
— Specific institution/official action challenged
— Compensation/specific relief for individual victim sought

Which Court: HC vs. SC

HC under Art 226 (Preferred for child cases):

— More accessible; faster hearings

— State-level systemic issues (CCI abuse, POCSO trial pendency)

— Can issue mandamus to state authorities (DM, SP, education minister)

— Can frame district-level rehabilitation protocols

SC under Art 32 (For nationwide issues):

— Multi-state systemic failure (e.g., POCSO trial delays nationally)

— Constitutional questions (e.g., validity of bail procedures in POCSO)

— Requires demonstration of larger public importance

Locus Standi in Child Rights PILs

Very liberal in child cases. Any person can file:

— Child's guardian (preferred)
— Child rights organization on behalf of child
— NGO with prior HC recognition
— Episotolary jurisdiction: Even letter to court constitutes PIL petition

Relief to Seek in Child Rights PIL

— Mandamus: Compel action (e.g., "DM shall designate CCI inspector within 2 weeks")
— Habeas Corpus: Release child from illegal detention in institution
— Prohibition: Stop ongoing abuse (e.g., "CCI staff shall not administer corporal punishment")
— Directions: Structural reforms (e.g., establish SJPU in each district by date X)
— Compensation: For affected children (interim/final)
— Contempt: If government violates PIL compliance order

Stay Applications in Child Protection PILs

Child cases receive expedited stay decisions:

— Interim relief granted quickly (e.g., stay on child's removal from CCI pending inquiry)
— Burden on government to justify inaction
— Child's best interest presumed in stay analysis

SECTION 4: Trial Strategy in POCSO Cases

Prosecution Angle: Building the Case

Medical Evidence (MLC):

— Medical examination primordial; capture within 72 hours (DNA, injury, trauma)

— Medical report corroborates child's version; also rebuts "consent" claim

— Genital injury/infection strongly supports case

Section 164 Statement:

— Recorded by trained officer; preferably female; at safe location

— Consistency between 164 statement and trial testimony is gold standard

— Retraction of 164 statement must be explained (Rishipal: retraction alone insufficient for acquittal)

Digital/CCTV Evidence:

— CCTV footage from school, neighborhood, accused's home

— WhatsApp/SMS messages (grooming patterns)

— Mobile location data (accused near victim at time of alleged assault)

Expert Testimony:

— Forensic expert on child trauma/abuse patterns

— Psychologist on victim's behavior (fear, shame-driven retraction)

— Medical expert on injury consistency with allegations

Prosecution: Child Witness Examination

— Competency test (Evidence Act Sec 118): Child must understand nature of oath, questions. Most children above 5-6 years pass.
— Leading questions not allowed: Child's narrative is paramount; open-ended questions preferred
— Court's protective role: Judge stops aggressive cross-examination; prevents secondary victimization
— Support person present: Mother/counselor in courtroom to ease child's anxiety

Prosecution: Dealing with Victim Retractions

Per Rishipal v. Uttarakhand, retraction is not fatal if explained:

— Why do victims retract? Pressure from accused/family, shame, economic dependence on accused, fear of social ostracism
— Strategy: Examine reasons for retraction. If pressure/fear, retraction loses value.
— Corroboration: Medical evidence, digital evidence, other witness testimony.
— Argument: "Retraction is expected in child sexual assault due to social pressure; but medical injury, 164 statement, and accused's proximity to victim on date corroborate original complaint."

Defence Angle: Fair Trial Advocacy

Challenge Age of Victim:

— Ossification test limitations (2-year margin of error); if test shows 17-19, argue child not below 18

— School records may be falsified; cross-examine school principal

— If child's age ambiguous, benefit to accused (JJ Act protections don't apply to adults)

Challenge Identification:

— Was child present at alleged location? Test Identification Parade.

— Dock identification by child (of accused in court) is weak; cross-examine on visibility at crime scene

— Mistaken identity: Child may confuse accused with another person

False Complaint (Section 22 POCSO):

— Is there motive for false complaint? (Custody dispute, property conflict?)

— Examine inconsistencies in child's testimony

— Section 22: Burden on accused to prove falsity with knowledge; but circumstantial evidence of motive helps

— Note: False complaint conviction rare; conviction for falsity requires very strong evidence

Bail Strategy for Defence:

— Challenge prosecution's case strength at bail stage

— Argue: Medical evidence weak, child's statement inconsistent, no corroboration

— Emphasize accused's ties to locality, no flight risk, no antecedents

— Oppose victim's opposition by proving falsity of allegations

SECTION 5: International Law Arguments

Using UNCRC Before Indian Courts

Per Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan, international conventions are interpretive aids (not binding but persuasive):

— Cite UNCRC Articles in arguments to strengthen child rights positions
— Example: "Article 3 of UNCRC requires child's best interests be paramount; this court should prioritize victim's safety over accused's bail"
— SC often references UNCRC when interpreting Constitution articles

Key UNCRC Articles for India Arguments

Article 3: Child's best interests shall be primary consideration
Article 12: Child's right to be heard in legal proceedings
Article 19: Protection from violence, abuse, neglect
Article 34: Protection from sexual exploitation and abuse

CRC Committee Observations on India

CRC Committee has issued periodic observations on India's child protection record; cite in PIL arguments:

— India must strengthen POCSO trial capacity
— Child Labour remediation remains weak
— CCI oversight needs strengthening
— SC can use these observations to justify PIL directions to government

Bangalore Principles on International Law

Principles on relationship of domestic and international law in argument:

— UNCRC is persuasive authority for interpreting ambiguous Constitution provisions
— When Indian law is silent, international norms can guide interpretation
— Does not substitute Indian law; merely informs judicial reasoning
