SECTION 1: POCSO Act 2012 — Complete Legislative Map

Pre-Legislative History

Sakshi v. UOI (2004) — Supreme Court found IPC inadequate for child sexual assault. This judgment led to POCSO enactment in 2012 as specialized legislation for child sexual abuse.

Definitional Framework

Section 2(d) — "child" means any person below 18 years. Includes both male and female children.

Section 3 — "Penetrative sexual assault" — Penetration of vagina/anus/mouth with penis/object/any body part with sexual intent. Punishment: 7-10 years, fine up to ₹15 lakhs.

Section 5 — "Aggravated penetrative sexual assault" — 17 aggravating circumstances (police, teacher, caregiver, acts causing grievous injury, etc.). Punishment: 10-14 years, fine up to ₹21 lakhs.

Section 7 — "Sexual assault" — Intentional touching/making child touch for sexual gratification. Punishment: 3-5 years, fine up to ₹10 lakhs.

Section 9 — "Aggravated sexual assault" — Similar aggravating factors. Punishment: 5-7 years, fine up to ₹15 lakhs.

Section 11 — "Sexual harassment" — Inappropriate remarks, physical conduct, demand for sexual favor. Punishment: 3 months-2 years, fine up to ₹6 lakhs.

Section 13 — "Pornography" — Producing/distributing/possessing child sexual abuse material (CSAM). Punishment: 3 months-3 years, fine up to ₹5 lakhs.

Section 15 — "Storing CSAM" — Punishment: 3 months-3 years, fine up to ₹5 lakhs.

Chapter IV: Procedures

Section 19 — Mandatory reporting by any person who knows of child sexual abuse. Failure to report is not criminalized but duty exists.

Section 20 — Media cannot identify child in news reporting.

Section 21 — Failure to report by professionals (doctors, teachers, officers) does not attract criminal punishment but violates duty.

Section 22 — False complaint with knowledge of falsity is punishable. Defense must prove knowledge of falsity; benefit of doubt goes to complainant.

Section 23 — Identity of child protected; use pseudonym in all proceedings.

Section 24 — Child's statement recorded by trained official; preferably female; in child-friendly manner.

Section 25 — Magistrate can record child's statement under CrPC Section 164; conducted by trained officer.

Section 26 — Medical examination of child with consent; refusal not to be held against child.

Chapter V: Special Courts

Section 28 — Designation of Special Courts by High Court; preferably one per district.

Section 29 — Presumption of guilt — Once child's testimony is found credible, presumption shifts to accused; accused must prove innocence. Major evidentiary advantage to prosecution.

Section 30 — Presumption re culpable mental state; if physical injury evident, presumption that accused knew child was below 18.

Section 31 — Special Court conduct trial; CrPC applies; but child-friendly modifications mandatory.

Section 33 — Child-friendly trial atmosphere; trained judges; protective measures during examination.

Section 35 — Trial within 1 year of FIR; compliance monitored by High Court.

Section 36 — Protection of child from accused during trial; separate entrance; protective screens.

Section 37 — In-camera trial mandatory; privacy of child protected.

Section 38 — Public Prosecutor for prosecution; Central government to provide.

Section 39 — Support person during trial; social worker/family member.

Chapter VI: Victim Compensation

Section 33(8) — Interim compensation; Special Court can direct state to pay pending trial.

POCSO Rules 2020 Rule 7 — Compensation guidelines; quantum depends on aggravation and injury.

POCSO Rules 2020 — Key Changes from 2012 Rules

— Extended support person role; can be present during all interactions with child

— SJPU (Special Juvenile Police Unit) protocols for child handling

— Child-friendly atmosphere in police stations; separate rooms for recording statements

— Video recording of child's statement mandatory; can be used in evidence

— Section 34: Child not to be called to police station; statement recorded at safe location

— Section 35: Trial within 1 year of FIR registration

SECTION 2: JJ Act 2015 — Complete with 2021 Amendment

2021 Amendment — Key Changes

(1) DM/ADM now have quasi-judicial powers for Child Care Institution oversight — previously only JJB/CWC

(2) Adoption orders now by District Magistrate, not Child Welfare Committee (though CWC prepares report)

(3) Definitions expanded — CNCP, CCL definitions broadened

(4) Stricter CCI registration — Fingerprint verification, character certificates mandatory; surprise inspections

CNCP — Child in Need of Care and Protection (Sec 2(14))

Includes 11 clauses: orphan, disabled, exploited, abused, POCSO victim, victim of trafficking, at risk of trafficking, substance abuse, no safe place, without education, extreme poverty.

CCL — Child in Conflict with Law (Sec 2(13))

Child involved in conduct constituting offence under criminal law; requires rehabilitation not punishment; focus on restoration.

CWC — Child Welfare Committee (Sec 27-40)

Powers: Inquiry into cases, issue orders for child's welfare. Composition: one chairperson + 4 members. Orders available: release to parent/guardian, placement in CCI, sponsorship, foster care, adoption.

JJB — Juvenile Justice Board (Sec 4-26)

Composition: one Magistrate + two social workers. Bail (Sec 12): Presumption of liberty; bail generally granted unless serious danger. Preliminary assessment (Sec 15): Nature of offense, child's background, rehabilitation potential. Orders (Sec 18): Probation, community service, transfer to court (for 16-18 year old in heinous crime), etc.

Child Care Institutions (Sec 50-73)

Types: Children's homes, open shelters, observation homes, special homes (for CCL), after-care centers. Registration now stricter post-2021. Inspection: Monthly by DM/ADM; Surprise inspections by JJB.

Rehabilitation (Sec 39-48)

Restoration: Return to family where safe. Foster care: Temporary arrangement. Sponsorship: Economic support while child remains at home. Adoption: Permanent alternative. After-care: Support post-18.

DCPU — District Child Protection Unit (Sec 106)

SIR (Social Investigation Report), ICP (Initial Care Plan), case management.

SECTION 3: Allied Legislation

Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act 2016

Complete ban below 14 years — No child labour permitted. Ban below 18 in hazardous occupation/processes (mining, manufacturing explosives, etc.). Exceptions: family business, entertainment, sports. Penalties enhanced: ₹1-3 lakhs fine, 3-5 years imprisonment.

Prohibition of Child Marriage Act (PCMA) 2006

Void vs. Voidable (Sec 3, 12): Child's marriage voided if either party below 18. Preventive injunction (Sec 13): District Judge can prevent threatened marriage. Role of Prohibition Officers: District administration appoints officers to prevent marriages.

Right to Free and Compulsory Education Act (RTE) 2009

Article 21A implementation — Education as fundamental right. Neighbourhood school concept: Child has right to school near residence. Section 12(1)(c) — 25% reservation in private schools for economically weaker sections. Section 17 — Prohibition on physical punishment; corporal punishment banned. No-detention policy — Child cannot be detained in same class; continuous evaluation.

CNCP-CCL Overlap

When POCSO victim is also in conflict with law (e.g., child victim who committed petty crime to survive): JJ Act principles apply; focus on rehabilitation; child's victim status takes precedence; special support arrangements.

SECTION 4: BNS/BNSS Provisions (New Criminal Laws)

Bhartiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) — Relevant Sections

Section 94 — Trafficking (general)

Section 95 — Trafficking of child for exploitation — Enhanced to 14 years minimum punishment (previously 7 years)

Section 96 — Exploitation of trafficked person

Sections 74, 75, 76 — Offences against women and children; rape and gang rape

Bhartiya Nagrik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) — Relevant Sections

Section 163 — Statement recording (former CrPC Section 161)

Section 183 — Magistrate statement (former Section 164)

Section 176 — Inquest (former Section 174)

Section 528 — Inherent powers (former Section 482 CrPC)

Transitional Provisions

Cases registered before July 1, 2024 continue under old IPC/CrPC. New cases from July 1, 2024 onwards under BNS/BNSS. Practitioners must be conversant with both regimes during transition period.
