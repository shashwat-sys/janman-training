🏛️ Public Interest Litigation for Child Rights

PIL is the most powerful tool for systemic child rights enforcement in India. Since Bandhua Mukti Morcha (1984), the SC has relaxed locus standi — any person acting bona fide in the public interest can file a PIL for child rights violations.

Locus Standi in Child Rights PILs

Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India
(1984) 3 SCC 161
Any member of public having sufficient interest can approach SC under Art 32 or HC under Art 226 for enforcement of fundamental rights of the poor. Letter to court treated as writ petition. Epistolary jurisdiction established.
PUDR v. Union of India
(1982) 3 SCC 235
For enforcement of rights under Art 21/23, third-party can file — court does not require the actual victim to be the petitioner. Extended access to justice for voiceless populations including children.

When to File PIL vs. Individual Writ

FactorPILIndividual Writ
IssueSystemic failure — many children affectedIndividual child's specific grievance
StandingAny person, NGO, lawyerAggrieved person or guardian
CourtHC (Art 226) or SC (Art 32)HC (Art 226) usually preferred
ReliefDirections to government; policy changes; monitoringSpecific mandamus, compensation, habeas corpus
TimeLonger — monitoring requiredCan be faster for urgent cases

Steps to File a Child Rights PIL

  1. Identify the systemic violation — POCSO pendency? CCI abuse? Child labour persistence? Child marriage? Lack of SJPU?
  2. Gather evidence — RTI data, media reports, NCPCR reports, HC reports, academic studies
  3. Draft petition — Facts, constitutional provisions violated, reliefs sought, urgency
  4. Choose court — State-level = High Court (Art 226); national = SC (Art 32)
  5. File with affidavit — Attach evidence; submit to HC/SC Registry
  6. Seek interim directions — If urgent, apply for interim mandamus at first hearing
  7. Monitoring — Participate in compliance reporting; engage amicus curiae if appointed

💰 Constitutional Tort — Compensation under Art 32/226

Rudul Sah v. State of Bihar
(1983) 4 SCC 141
First case recognising right to compensation under Art 32 for violation of fundamental rights by state. SC held that power under Art 32 to issue directions includes power to award compensation. State cannot commit constitutional wrong and escape liability. Child rights violations by state can attract Rudul Sah damages.
Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa
(1993) 2 SCC 746
SC awarded compensation for custodial death under Art 32/226. Distinguished from civil suit — public law remedy. State vicariously liable for acts of its officers. Constitutional tort doctrine firmly established. Applicable to cases of children dying or being harmed in institutional care (CCIs, observation homes).
For child rights cases: If a child dies or is harmed in a government CCI (observation home/children's home), constitutional tort under Nilabati is available directly in HC under Art 226. No need to wait for criminal conviction.

Art 32 vs. Art 226 for Child Rights

Strategic Comparison
Art 32 (SC): Can award monetary compensation; broader reach for national issues; constitutional tort fully developed. Art 226 (HC): Wider scope — can enforce statutory rights, not just fundamental rights; faster access; can compel DM/state government action on POCSO/JJ Act failures; territorial jurisdiction.

🌍 International Law in Indian Courts

The Vishaka Approach

Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan
(1997) 6 SCC 241
In absence of domestic legislation on sexual harassment, SC used CEDAW (international convention) as interpretive aid and issued binding guidelines. Established that international conventions ratified by India can be used as interpretive tools for Art 14, 19, 21 even without domestic legislation implementing them.

Using UNCRC in Child Rights Arguments

India ratified the UNCRC in 1992. Key provisions for litigation:

Cite CRC Committee General Comments in PIL arguments. CRC Committee General Comment No. 13 (Violence against Children) and No. 25 (Digital Environment) are particularly powerful for POCSO-related arguments. Also cite CRC Committee's Concluding Observations on India's periodic reports.

⚖️ Bail Jurisprudence in Child Cases

Bail in POCSO Cases

POCSO has no specific bail restriction unlike NDPS, but courts exercise caution given the gravity of offences and child victim's vulnerability.

Asha Ranjan v. State of Bihar
(2017) 4 SCC 397
Victim (or parent/guardian acting for child victim) has a right to oppose bail application. Victim's counsel can appear at bail hearings. Court must consider: (a) victim's safety if bail granted; (b) risk of witness tampering; (c) accused's proximity to victim; (d) nature of offence.

Factors for Bail in Child Cases

  • Severity of offence — aggravated penetrative sexual assault: courts very reluctant to grant bail
  • Accused's relationship to victim — family member/teacher/neighbour: heightened witness tampering risk
  • Risk of further abuse if bail granted
  • Whether trial is progressing — prolonged incarceration without trial also violates Art 21
  • Medical evidence: strong MLC evidence weighs against bail
Anticipatory Bail in POCSO
Available under Section 438 CrPC/BNSS 482. Courts increasingly reluctant to grant in heinous POCSO offences. If granted, strict conditions: no contact with victim/family, surrender passport, report to police station weekly, etc.

🎭 Moot Scenario: "State of Bihar v. X"

Moot Problem: Child Rights — Multiple Issues
For Moot Court / Academic Exercise
Facts: X, a 35-year-old teacher at a private school in Patna, is accused of committing sexual assault (Section 7/8 POCSO) on "Y," a 13-year-old female student. FIR registered. X applies for bail. Medical examination done by male doctor (mother had refused woman doctor). Section 164 statement recorded at police station, not at child's residence. X claims Y's age is actually 14.5 years and the alleged act did not involve skin-to-skin contact, hence not "sexual assault."

Issues for Advocates:
(1) Was medical examination procedurally valid? (No — woman doctor mandatory for girl child under Section 27 POCSO)
(2) Was Section 164 statement properly recorded? (No — must be at child's residence/place of choice, not police station)
(3) Does "no skin-to-skin contact" provide any defence? (No — AG v. Satish 2021 settled this)
(4) How to determine age — no birth certificate? (Abuzar Hossain methodology — school records first)
(5) Should bail be granted? (Asha Ranjan — victim's right to oppose; teacher-student power dynamic; risk to child)
(6) Identity of child disclosed in media report — what remedy? (Nipun Saxena; Section 23 POCSO; FIR against reporter)
