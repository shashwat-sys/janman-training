Constitutional & Statutory Framework for Child Rights

Foundational legal provisions protecting children in India

1. Constitutional Provisions

Article 15(3): Special Provisions for Women and Children
Article 15(3) grants Parliament power to make special laws for women and children. This is an enabling provision that allows protective legislation.
Key Concept: Article 15(3) is the constitutional basis for gender-specific and child-protective laws like PCMA 2006 and provisions in POCSO Act.
Article 21: Right to Life and Dignity
The right to life extends beyond mere survival to include a dignified life. The Supreme Court has progressively expanded this right through landmark judgments.
Unnikrishnan v. State of AP (1993) — Right to education flows from Article 21, applicable to children aged 6-14.
Gaurav Jain v. UOI (1997) — Children of sex workers have a right to dignified life and education under Article 21 and 39(f).
Article 21A: Free and Compulsory Education (44th Amendment, 2002)
The State shall provide free and compulsory education to all children of the age of six to fourteen years. This constitutional mandate became the foundation of the Right to Free and Compulsory Education Act, 2009.
Statutory Consequence: The RTE Act 2009 operationalizes Article 21A. Private unaided non-minority schools must reserve 25% seats for disadvantaged children.
Article 23: Prohibition of Traffic in Human Beings and Forced Labour
Traffic in human beings and forced labour are prohibited. Violation is a criminal offense. Children are uniquely vulnerable to trafficking and forced labour exploitation.
NHRC v. State of Arunachal Pradesh (1996) — State has constitutional duty to prevent child trafficking and provide rehabilitation.
Article 24: Prohibition of Child Labour in Hazardous Employment
No child below 15 years shall be employed in any factory, mine, or in any other hazardous occupation. This is an absolute prohibition, not subject to reasonable restrictions.
M.C. Mehta v. State of Tamil Nadu (1996) — Hazardous child labour violates Article 24. Court directed elimination of child labour in match and cracker industries in Sivakasi.
Articles 39(e) and 39(f): Directive Principles
Article 39(e) requires the State to ensure that childhood and youth are protected against exploitation and moral and material abandonment. Article 39(f) obligates protection of children against economic exploitation.
Interpretive Tool: DPSP are not directly enforceable but serve as interpretive aids for Articles 14, 19, and 21. Courts increasingly use DPSP to enlarge rights of children.
Article 51A(k): Fundamental Duty
Every parent/guardian shall provide education to child between 6-14 years. This fundamental duty complements Article 21A as a constitutional obligation.
Enforcement: While not directly actionable, Article 51A(k) is used to justify state action against parents failing to send children to school.

2. International Law Framework

United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC), 1989
India ratified UNCRC in 1992 without reservations on substantive provisions. UNCRC defines a child as person below 18 years and establishes 4 pillars: survival, development, protection, and participation.
Key Articles: Art 1 (definition), Art 3 (best interests), Art 12 (right to be heard), Art 19 (protection from violence), Art 34 (protection from sexual exploitation).
Optional Protocol on Sale of Children, Child Prostitution and Child Pornography (2000)
India ratified in 2009. Strengthens child protection against sexual exploitation and commercial abuse. Criminalizes production, distribution, and possession of child sexual abuse material.
ILO Convention No. 182 on Worst Forms of Child Labour
India ratified in 1999. Requires elimination of worst forms of child labour including slavery, forced labour, hazardous work, and sexual exploitation of children.
Use of UNCRC in Indian Courts
Following the Vishaka approach (1997), Indian courts increasingly rely on UNCRC principles to interpret Indian law and expand child rights. The Supreme Court treats UNCRC as interpretive aid for Articles 14, 19, 21.
Bachpan Bachao Andolan (2011) — Used UNCRC principles on child trafficking and protection to direct comprehensive state action.
M.C. Mehta (1996) — Applied UNCRC standards on child labour to interpret Article 24 expansively.

3. Statutory Framework Overview

Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012 + 2019 Amendment
Gender-neutral statute that criminalizes all forms of sexual abuse against children (persons below 18). Key features: strict liability (no age exception), special courts, child-friendly procedure, in-camera trials, identity protection.
2019 Amendment: Added provisions on child pornography, child grooming, and aggravated forms of sexual assault with enhanced penalties.
Juvenile Justice Act (Care and Protection of Children) Rules, 2015 + 2021 Amendment
Applies "best interests of the child" standard in all proceedings affecting juveniles. Distinguishes between children in conflict with law (JCL) and children in need of care and protection (CNCP). Mandates reformative rather than punitive approach.
2021 Amendment: Transferred CCI registration powers from JJB to District Magistrate. Strengthened CCI inspections and monitoring.
Prohibition of Child Marriage Act (PCMA), 2006
Replaced outdated Child Marriage Restraint Act of 1929. Makes child marriage void (not merely voidable). Recognizes marriage of boys below 21 and girls below 18 as void ab initio.
Independent Thought v. UOI (2017) — Court struck down Exception 2 to Section 375 IPC that exempted marital rape of girls 15-17 years, aligning with PCMA's protection.
Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2016
Prohibits employment of children below 14 in hazardous industries. Amendment raised minimum age for light work from 13 to 14 years. Requires registration of CCIs and training of inspectors.
Right to Free and Compulsory Education (RTE) Act, 2009
Makes education a fundamental right. Mandates free and compulsory education for all children aged 6-14. Requires private unaided non-minority schools to reserve 25% seats for disadvantaged children (Section 12(1)(c)).
Society for Unaided Private Schools v. UOI (2012) — Supreme Court upheld constitutionality of 25% reservation in private schools.
Protection of Children from Sexual Offences Rules, 2020
Procedural rules implementing POCSO Act. Establish special investigation units, victim support schemes, guidelines for child-friendly trial procedures, and compensation framework.

4. Legal Principles in Child Law

Best Interests of the Child
This international standard, originating from UNCRC Article 3, has been codified in Indian law through Section 2(9) of the Juvenile Justice Act. Every decision affecting a child must prioritize the child's best interests.
Application: Courts must balance child's welfare against other considerations (including state interests) in custody, guardianship, and protection matters.
Child-Friendly Procedures
POCSO Act mandates child-friendly procedures including in-camera trials, use of screens/closed-circuit television, presence of support person, and minimal cross-examination.
Constitutional Obligation: Courts recognize child-friendly procedure as a facet of Article 21 right to dignity and fair trial.
Parens Patriae Doctrine
The state acts as parent (parens patriae) when a child's welfare is jeopardized. This doctrine originated in English common law but has constitutional roots in Article 39(f) and Article 21.
Application: Justifies state intervention in rescue, rehabilitation, adoption, and institutional care of vulnerable children.
Age Determination Jurisprudence
When child's age is disputed, courts must apply protective presumptions. The issue is critical in determining jurisdiction (JJB vs. criminal court) and applicability of child protection laws.
Pratap Singh v. State of Jharkhand (2005) — Age must be determined as on date of offense, not date of trial. Five-Judge Constitutional Bench judgment.
Abuzar Hossain v. State of WB (2012) — Ossification test not sole determinant. Benefit of doubt (2-year margin) given in favor of accused claiming to be minor.
